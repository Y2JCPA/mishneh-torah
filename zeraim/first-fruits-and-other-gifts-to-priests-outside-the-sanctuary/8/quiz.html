🎓 Quiz

הלכות ביכורים פרק ח · 5 Questions
Question 1
A man separates flour (before any water is added) and gives it to a priest as 'challah.' What is the law?
Challah cannot be separated from flour — only from 'your dough' (Numbers 15:20). The flour is considered stolen property in the priest's hands and must be returned, and challah must still be separated from the remaining dough.
Question 2
From what point is a person permitted to begin separating challah, even though it is not yet the ideal time?
Once water is mixed with flour, one may separate challah if worried the dough will become ritually impure. The ideal is to wait until fully kneaded (when wheat is rolled into a ball), but early separation is permitted as a safeguard.
Question 3
A gentile gives a Jew flour to knead into dough, then gives the finished dough as a gift. In which scenario must the Jew separate challah?
The obligation depends on who owns the dough at the moment it is rolled into a ball. If the gift was made before rolling, the obligation takes effect while it belongs to the Jew — he is liable. If gifted after rolling, the obligation would have applied when it still belonged to the gentile — it is exempt.
Question 4
After a dough has already been rolled into a ball, a doubt arises about whether it contracted Scriptural-level ritual impurity. How should the challah be treated?
Once the challah obligation has taken effect (post-rolling), we do not deliberately cause it to become impure. The challah is separated but left in suspension: not eaten (lest it be impure) and not burnt (lest it turn out to be pure and we destroy it needlessly).
Question 5
A buyer purchases bread from a common-person baker in Syria who says 'I separated challah.' What must the buyer do?
Just as the entire Jewish people in Eretz Yisrael are not suspected of neglecting the separation of terumah, Jews in Syria are not suspected of neglecting challah. When the baker in Syria states he has separated challah, the buyer may rely on that claim without further action.
