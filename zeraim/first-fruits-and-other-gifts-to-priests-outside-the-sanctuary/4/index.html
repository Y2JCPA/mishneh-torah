Two Tiers of Obligation: Bringing vs. Declaring

הלכות א׳–ג׳
הלכה א׳
כָּל הַמֵּבִיא בִּכּוּרִים טָעוּן קָרְבָּן וְשִׁיר וּתְנוּפָה וְלִינָה. אֲבָל הַוִּדּוּי אֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה בַּכּל. לְפִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ שֶׁחַיָּבִין לְהָבִיא בִּכּוּרִים וְאֵינָן קוֹרִין עֲלֵיהֶם:
Whenever anyone brings the first fruits, it is necessary that a [peace] offering be brought, song [be recited], they be raised [up and down and to all four directions] and the person remain in Jerusalem overnight.1As explained in the concluding halachot of the previous chapter. The declaration, however, is not a universal requirement. There are some who are obligated to bring the first fruits, but who do not recite the declaration over them.
הלכה ב׳
וְאֵלּוּ מְבִיאִין וְלֹא קוֹרִין. הָאִשָּׁה וְהַטֻּמְטוּם וְהָאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוּס לְפִי שֶׁהֵן סְפֵק אִשָּׁה וְאֵינָן יְכוֹלִין לוֹמַר אֲשֶׁר נָתַתָּ לִּי ה'. וְכֵן הָאַפּוֹטְרוֹפִּין וְהָעֶבֶד וְהַשָּׁלִיחַ לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָן יְכוֹלִין לוֹמַר (דברים כו י) "אֲשֶׁר נָתַתָּ לִּי ה'":
The following must bring [the first fruits], but may not recite the declaration: a woman, a tumtum2A person whose genital area is covered with a mass of flesh and whose gender is impossible to detect. and an androgynous3A person with male and female sexual characteristics. - for there is a doubt whether they are considered as a female;4With regard to an androgynus, the doubt concerns the individual's halachic status. With regard to the tumtum, the doubt concerns the actual facts: Which gender is covered by the mass of flesh? none of these can say "[the land] which You gave me,"5In his Commentary to the Mishnah (Bikkurim 1:5), the Rambam explains that Eretz Yisrael was given as an ancestral heritage only to males who are free men. Thus this disqualifies all of those mentioned in this clause and also the servant mentioned in the following clause. and similarly, a guardian, a servant, and an agent, because they cannot say "which You gave me."6For the first fruits they are bringing are not from their own land. They are acting on behalf of others.
הלכה ג׳
הַגֵּר מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר לְאַבְרָהָם (בראשית יז ה) "אַב הֲמוֹן גּוֹיִם נְתַתִּיךָ" הֲרֵי הוּא אַב כָּל הָעוֹלָם כֻּלּוֹ שֶׁנִּכְנָסִין תַּחַת כַּנְפֵי שְׁכִינָה. וּלְאַבְרָהָם הָיְתָה הַשְּׁבוּעָה תְּחִלָּה שֶׁיִּירְשׁוּ בָּנָיו אֶת הָאָרֶץ. וְכֵן כֹּהֲנִים וּלְוִיִּם מְבִיאִין וְקוֹרִין מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן עָרֵי מִגְרָשׁ:
A convert, by contrast, may bring the first fruits and make the declaration, for [Genesis 17:5] states with regard to Abraham: "I have made you a father to a multitude of nations." Implied is that he is the father of all those who enter under the shelter of the Divine presence.7For he taught all mankind faith [the Rambam's Commentary to the Mishnah (loc. cit.:4)]. The oath that his descendants would inherit the land was given to Abraham first.8I.e., all future converts are considered as Abraham's seed and thus have a share in the land "that God swore to [give] to your ancestors." Indeed, based on Ezekiel 47:22, Kapot Temarim states that in the Messianic era, converts will be given a share of the Holy Land. Similarly, the priest and the Levites bring [first fruits] and make a declaration, because they have cities in which to dwell.9As related in Numbers, ch. 35, in addition to the six cities of refuge, the Levites were given 42 cities that were scattered throughout the entire land of Eretz Yisrael. Although the land was given them from the tribal inheritances of the other tribes, it is still appropriate for them to speak of the land which God gave them, because the gifts were given as a result of God's command (Radbaz). See also Hilchot Ma'aser Sheni 11:17.
🌿 Core Distinction
Every valid bringer must bring with offering, song, waving, and an overnight stay — but the verbal declaration is a separate, higher standard. Women, tumtum, androgynous, guardians, servants, and agents all bring without declaring because they cannot honestly say 'the land You gave me.' A convert, however, both brings and declares — Abraham is father to all who enter under God's presence, making the covenant theirs too.

Ownership Complications: Partial Rights and Sold Fields

הלכות ד׳–ח׳
הלכה ד׳
הַקּוֹנֶה שְׁנֵי אִילָנוֹת בְּתוֹךְ [שָׂדֶה] שֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא לְפִי שֶׁהַדָּבָר סָפֵק אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ קַרְקַע אוֹ אֵין לוֹ. וְכֵיצַד עוֹשֶׂה. מַקְדִּישׁ אוֹתָם תְּחִלָּה לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן סְפֵק חֻלִּין וְאֵין מַכְנִיסִין חֻלִּין לַעֲזָרָה וְהַכֹּהֵן פּוֹדֶה אוֹתָן מִיַּד הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ וְאַחַר כָּךְ אוֹכְלָן. וּמַפְרִישׁ מֵהֶן תְּרוּמָה וּמַעֲשֵׂר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן סְפֵק חֻלִּין וְנוֹתֵן הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת שֶׁלָּהֶן לַכֹּהֲנִים שֶׁמָּא בִּכּוּרִים הֵם וַאֲסוּרִין לְזָרִים. וְאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אוֹתָן בְּעַצְמוֹ אֶלָּא מְשַׁלְּחָן בְּיַד שָׁלִיחַ כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּעַכֵּב אוֹתָן הַקְּרִיאָה מִלְּאָכְלָן. שֶׁכָּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לִקְרִיאָה מִפְּנֵי הַסָּפֵק הַקְּרִיאָה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ:
A person who purchases two trees in his colleague's field10He does not purchase the land on which the trees grow. Nevertheless, since he purchased two trees, our Sages (Bava Batra 81b; see Hilchot Mechirah 24:6) question whether or not he is given the right to the land around the trees. If, however, he purchases three trees, all agree that he has the right to the land. must bring [the first fruits], but may not recite the declaration.11Bava Batra 82a questions: Reciting the declaration is no more than reciting verses from the Torah. Even if he is not obligated to do so, what would be wrong with reciting these verses? In resolution, it explains that a) it appears that he is lying (because he is praising God for giving him land which in fact may not be his); or b) by bringing them as first fruits, he will prevent the terumah and the tithes from being separated from them. [The rationale is that] there is a doubt whether he has a right to the land [on which the trees stand] or not.What should he do? He should first12I.e., before bringing them to the Temple Courtyard. consecrate them to the Temple treasury13The consecration has a questionable status, because a person may not consecrate property that does not belong to him (Hilchot Arachin VaCharamim 6:16). And if the produce is first fruits, it does not belong to him. for it is possible that they are ordinary produce14For if the land does not belong to him, they are not considered as first fruits. and non-consecrated food is not brought into the Temple Courtyard.15This is a general principle. See Hilchot Shechitah 2:3. The priest then redeems them from the Temple treasury and partakes of them.He must separate terumah and the tithes from them, because of the possibility that they are ordinary produce. He must give the tithes from them to the priests,16According to Scriptural Law, tithes must be given to the Levites. Nevertheless, when the Levites failed to return to Zion with Ezra in large numbers, he punished them and decreed that they should not be given the tithes. Afterwards, according to many views, it was possible to give the tithes either to the priests or the Levites. Nevertheless, in this instance, the tithes must be given to the priests for the reason stated by the Rambam. because it is a possibility that they are first fruits and forbidden to non-priests. He should not bring them himself, but instead, send them via an agent17For when an agent brings first fruits, a declaration should not be made, as stated in Halachah 2. so that [the fact] that the declaration [is not recited] will not prevent them from being eaten. For whenever a declaration concerning [first fruits] is not fit to be made because of a doubt concerning their status, [the fact that] a declaration [is not recited] prevents it [from being eaten].18Bava Batra, loc. cit., explains that when there would be no difficulty in making the declaration, the fact that it is not made does not disqualify the first fruits from being eaten. When, however, it is required to be made, but cannot be made for a particular reason, the failure to make it disqualifies the offering.
הלכה ה׳
הִפְרִישׁ בִּכּוּרָיו וּמָכַר שָׂדֵהוּ מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא. שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לוֹמַר (דברים כו י) "אֲשֶׁר נָתַתָּ לִּי ה'" שֶׁהֲרֵי אֵין לוֹ. וְהַלּוֹקֵחַ אֵינוֹ חַיָּב לְהַפְרִישׁ בִּכּוּרִים אֲחֵרִים מֵאוֹתוֹ הַמִּין שֶׁכְּבָר הִפְרִישׁ מִמֶּנּוּ הַמּוֹכֵר. וְאִם הִפְרִישׁ מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא. אֲבָל מִמִּין אַחֵר מַפְרִישׁ וּמֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא:
If one set aside his first fruits and then sold his field, he should bring them, but not make the declaration, because he cannot say: "[the land] which You gave me,"19Praising God for giving him the land. since it no longer belongs to him. The purchaser is not required to separate other first fruits, because the seller has already separated them.20And first fruits should be brought only once for each species. In his Commentary to the Mishnah (Bikkurim 1:7, based on the Jerusalem Talmud), the Rambam states that this is derived from Deuteronomy 26:3 which states: "I am making a statement to God your Lord today," implying that the statement can be made only once (see also Halachah 11). If, however, he does separate them, he should bring them, but not make the declaration.21For the concept derived above applies primarily to making the declaration and not actually bringing the first fruits. From another species [of produce], he should set aside the first fruits, bring them, and make the declaration.22Since they are being brought by two individuals, the fact that one species has been brought already does not disqualify the first fruits brought by the other person. If, however, they were being brought by the same person, he would not make the declaration again, as stated in Halachah 11.
הלכה ו׳
הַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֵהוּ לְפֵרוֹת הַלּוֹקֵחַ מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא שֶׁקִּנְיַן פֵּרוֹת אֵינוֹ כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף. אֲבָל מֵבִיא אָדָם מִנִּכְסֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ בִּכּוּרִים וְקוֹרֵא אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ גּוּף הַקַּרְקַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (דברים כו יא) "אֲשֶׁר נָתַן לְךָ ה' אֱלֹהֶיךָ וּלְבֵיתֶךָ". אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמֵּתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ אַחַר שֶׁהִפְרִישׁ וְהוּא בַּדֶּרֶךְ מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא:
When a person sells [the right to harvest] the produce of his field, the purchaser should bring [the first fruits], but not make the declaration. [The rationale is that] the acquisition of the produce is not equivalent to the acquisition of [the land] itself.23And thus he cannot rightfully praise God for giving him the land. When, however, one brings first fruits from the property of his wife,24This refers even to property which the husband receives as nichsei milog (see Hilchot Ishut 16:1-2), i.e., the property itself belongs to the woman, but he is entitled to derive benefit from it. he makes the declaration even though he does not own the actual land, as [implied by Deuteronomy 26:11]: "which God, your Lord, has given you and your household."25"Your household" can be interpreted as referring to one's wife. Thus even though the land belongs to his wife, he should make the declaration. Even if his wife dies after he has set aside [the first fruits] and he is in the midst of his journey [to Jerusalem], he should bring [the first fruits] and make the declaration.26The husband inherits his wife's property. Thus now he owns both the land and the produce.
הלכה ז׳
הַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֵהוּ אוֹ שֶׁמָּכַר אִילָנוֹת וְקַרְקָעָן בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַיּוֹבֵל נוֹהֵג הֲרֵי זֶה מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא בְּיוֹבֵל רִאשׁוֹן בִּלְבַד שֶׁעֲדַיִן לֹא סָמְכָה דַּעְתּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹכֵר שֶׁתַּחֲזֹר לוֹ הַקַּרְקַע. אֲבָל אִם חָזַר וּמְכָרָהּ בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי הֲרֵי זֶה מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא שֶׁהֲרֵי סָמְכָה דַּעְתּוֹ שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא הַפֵּרוֹת וְקִנְיַן פֵּרוֹת אֵינוֹ כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף:
When a person sells his field or sells trees and the land around them in the era when the Jubilee year is observed,27And ancestral property is returned to its original owner. See Hilchot Shemitah VeYoval, ch. 11. [the purchaser] should bring [the first fruits] and make the declaration in the first Jubilee.28For he is the owner of the land and the produce. Although he is required to return the land in the Jubilee year, that is a result of the Torah's decree and does not diminish the purchaser's ownership of the land. [The rationale is that] the seller has not taken for granted that the land will be returned to him.29Since the owner does not have the experience of having his land returned to him, he does not consider it a surety that it will be returned to him. In his mind, it is sold (Radbaz). The Rambam's source is Gittin 48a. Rashi interprets the passage differently. If, however, he sold it again in the second Jubilee, [the purchaser] should bring [the first fruits], but should not make the declaration. For he is certain that he will receive only the produce,30And he knows that the land will be returned. Accordingly, it is as if he purchased only the produce and not the land. and the acquisition of the produce is not equivalent to the acquisition of [the land] itself.31As stated in Halachah 6.
הלכה ח׳
הִפְרִישׁ בִּכּוּרִים וְחַלָּה וַהֲרֵי הוּא מְסֻכָּן. זֶה שֶׁרָאוּי לְיָרְשׁוֹ מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא. הִפְרִישׁ בִּכּוּרָיו וְשִׁלְּחָן בְּיַד אַחֵר וּמֵת הַשָּׁלִיחַ בַּדֶּרֶךְ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחָזַר הוּא וֶהֱבִיאָן אֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (דברים כו ב) "וְלָקַחְתָּ" (דברים כו ג) "וּבָאתָ" עַד שֶׁתִּהְיֶה לְקִיחָה וַהֲבָאָה כְּאַחַת:
When a person set aside his first fruits and became mortally ill,32This reflects the Rambam's version of the Jerusalem Talmud (Bikkurim 1:5). The version in the standard published text is slightly different (Radbaz, Kessef Mishneh). the one who is fit to inherit his [estate] should bring the first fruits without making the declaration.33While the owner of the produce is alive, the person fit to inherit the estate is considered as an agent, and hence may not make the declaration. Even though he was not explicitly appointed, we assume that this is the owner's desire. According to the Rambam's conception, if the owner is ill, but is not in mortal danger, another person cannot serve as his agent unless he is appointed as such. When a person set aside his first fruits and sent them with an agent, but the agent died in the midst of the journey, even if the owner brought them, he should not make the delaration. [The rationale is that the phrase (Deuteronomy 26:2 :] "And you shall take... and you shall bring" does not apply unless the taking and the bringing are as one.
🏡 Land & Ownership
The declaration hinges on actual land ownership. Buying two trees without the land, selling a field after separation, purchasing only the harvest rights, or selling during Jubilee — all create scenarios where one brings but cannot declare. A husband bringing from his wife's property is the notable exception: 'which God gave you and your household' includes spousal land. The rule throughout: when ownership is in doubt or incomplete, bring — but stay silent.
5/8

When the Declaration Is Withheld: Loss, Impurity, and Timing

הלכות ט׳–י״ג
הלכה ט׳
הִפְרִישׁ בִּכּוּרָיו וְאָבְדוּ קֹדֶם שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְהַר הַבַּיִת וְהִפְרִישׁ אֲחֵרִים תַּחְתֵּיהֶן מֵבִיא הַשְּׁנִיִּים וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא. לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לוֹמַר אֶת (דברים כו ב) "רֵאשִׁית כָּל פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה" לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָן רֵאשִׁית. וְאֵלּוּ הַשְּׁנִיִּים אֵין חַיָּבִים עֲלֵיהֶן חֹמֶשׁ כְּבִכּוּרִים:
When a person set aside his first fruits, but they were lost34In any manner, as indicated by Chapter 2, Halachah 20. before they reached the Temple Mount and therefore he separated others instead of them,35For he is required to replace them (ibid.). he should bring the second fruits, but should not make the declaration. [The rationale is that] he cannot say [that he is bringing] "the first fruits of the land," for those that he is bringing are not the first fruits. One is not liable for an additional fifth [for partaking of] the produce separated second, as [he would had he partaken of] the first fruits.36As stated in Chapter 3, Halachah 5, the first fruits are considered as terumah. And as stated in Hilchot Terumah 6:6, one who partakes of terumah inadvertently must make restitution and add a fifth.
הלכה י׳
הֵבִיא בִּכּוּרָיו וְנִטְמְאוּ בַּעֲזָרָה נוֹפְצָם שָׁם וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא:
When a person brings his first fruits and they become impure in the Temple Courtyard, he should spill out the basket there.37Our translation is based on the Rambam's Commentary to the Mishnah (Bikkurim 1:8). The Ra'avad maintains that the produce should be left in the Temple Courtyard until it rots. From Chapter 2, Halachah 19, some have inferred that according to the Rambam, the impure produce should be burnt. Kin'at Eliyahu maintains that this approach is far more appropriate than leaving it to rot in the Temple Courtyard. He does not make the delaration.38For the declaration is made as a statement of thanksgiving after the first fruits are successfully offered and that is not possible in the present situation.
הלכה י״א׳
הֵבִיא בִּכּוּרָיו מֵאֶחָד מִן הַמִּינִים וְקָרָא וְחָזַר וְהֵבִיא בִּכּוּרִים מִמִּין אַחֵר אֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא עֲלֵיהֶן שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (דברים כו ג) "הִגַּדְתִּי הַיּוֹם" פַּעַם אַחַת בְּשָׁנָה הוּא מַגִּיד וְלֹא שְׁתַּיִם:
If a person brought his first fruits from one species and made the declaration and afterwards brings first fruits from another species, he should not make the declaration for them. [This is derived from the phrase (Deuteronomy 26:3 :] "I am making a statement today." [Implied is that] he makes one statement and not two.
הלכה י״ב׳
הִפְרִישׁ בִּכּוּרָיו וְיָבַשׁ הַמַּעְיָן אוֹ שֶׁנִּקְצַץ הָאִילָן מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא לְפִי שֶׁזֶּה כְּמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קַרְקַע שֶׁהֲרֵי אָבְדָה:
If a person separated his first fruits and then the spring [that served as his field's water supply]39The bracketed additions are based on the Rambam's Commentary to the Mishnah (Bikkurim 1:6). dried up or the tree [from which they were reaped] was cut down, he should bring them, but should not make the declaration. [The rationale is that] he is like someone who no longer possesses the land, because it has been ruined.40In its present state, the land is not able to produce fruit. Hence, it is as if he no longer possesses it (ibid.; according to Rav Kappach's text).
הלכה י״ג׳
הַמֵּבִיא בִּכּוּרִים מֵאַחַר חַג הַסֻּכּוֹת וְעַד חֲנֻכָּה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהִפְרִישָׁן קֹדֶם הֶחָג מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (דברים כו יא) "וְשָׂמַחְתָּ בְכָל הַטּוֹב" וְאֵין קְרִיאָה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁעַת שִׂמְחָה מֵחַג הַשָּׁבוּעוֹת עַד סוֹף הֶחָג. וּשְׁאָר הַמְּבִיאִין חוּץ מֵאֵלּוּ מְבִיאִין וְקוֹרִין:
When a person brings his first fruits between Sukkot and Chanukah41After Chanukah, he may not bring them at all.- even if he set them aside before Sukkot - he should bring them without making the declaration, [as implied by Deuteronomy 26:11]: "You shall rejoice in all the good." [Implied is that] the declaration should be made only in a time of happiness, [i.e.,] between Shavuot and the end of Sukkot.42This is the harvest season when a person feels happy with the bounty God has granted him. All individuals other than these43I.e., those mentioned from Halachah 5 and onward. must bring their first fruits and make the declaration.
🤐 Silent Offerings
Even a properly separated first-fruit can lose its declaration. If the original fruits were lost and replaced, the replacements are brought silently — they are not truly 'the first of the land.' Impurity in the Courtyard, an agent dying mid-journey, a spring drying up after separation, or bringing between Sukkot and Chanukah (outside the season of joy) all trigger silent bringing. The declaration belongs to a moment of wholeness: intact produce, intact ownership, intact joy.

הלכה י״ד׳
הַבִּכּוּרִים וְהַתְּרוּמוֹת וְהַחַלָּה וְהַקֶּרֶן וְהַחֹמֶשׁ וּמַתְּנוֹת בְּהֵמָה הֵם נִכְסֵי כֹּהֵן. יֵשׁ לוֹ לִקְנוֹת מֵהֶן עֲבָדִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת וּבְהֵמָה טְמֵאָה וּבַעַל חוֹב נוֹטְלָן בְּחוֹבוֹ וְהָאִשָּׁה בִּכְתֻבָּתָהּ. וְקוֹנֶה בָּהֶן סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה:
The first fruits, the terumot,44I.e., both "the great terumah" and terumat ma'aser. challah, the principal and the fifth,45I.e, when a non-priest partakes of any of the above mentioned substances, he is obligated to pay the principal and a fifth as restitution. and the presents given when slaughtering an animal46See Chapter 9. are a priest's private property. הלכה ט״ו׳

The first fruits and the terumot are forbidden to be eaten by non-priests. [Generally,] when the first fruits are mixed with ordinary produce, they are permitted if there is 101 times the original amount as is the law with regard to terumah.49Hilchot Ma'achalot Asuurot 15:3; Hilchot Terumot 15:20-21. Nevertheless, if they become mixed with the same species50If, however, they become mixed with a substance of another species, the presence of the first fruits can be nullified. See Hilchot Ma'achalot Assurot 15:12. of ordinary produce in Jerusalem, [the mixture] is forbidden even if the slightest amount of [the first fruits are mixed in] as [is the law regarding] the second tithe.51See Hilchot Ma'aser Sheni 6:14-15. [The rationale is that] since [they are located in Jerusalem,] the place where one partakes of them, they are considered like52This refers even to first fruits belonging to an Israelite. Hence, they are considered only "like an entity that could be permitted," but are not actually in that category (Radbaz). an entity that could become permitted.53For the entire mixture could be eaten by priests in a state of ritual purity. With regard to the law that the entire mixture is forbidden when even the slightest amount of a forbidden substance becomes mixed with it, see Hilchot Ma'achalot Assurot 15:10. Although the first fruits are forbidden to non-priests even in Jerusalem, nevertheless, even the slightest presence of them in a mixture causes it to be forbidden.Even if one sowed the first fruits after they were brought to Jerusalem,54And thus the forbidden fruit itself no longer exists. even the slightest amount of produce that grows from them causes a mixture to become forbidden.55This is a penalty imposed upon him because the mixture could have - and the produce that grows could - be given to the priests. If, however, one sows the first fruits before they were brought to Jerusalem, the produce that grows from them is ordinary produce.56Since the first fruits may not be eaten outside of Jerusalem, the mixture is not judged with the severity that applies to a mixture containing a forbidden entity that could become permitted. הלכה ט״ז׳
כֵּיצַד מַעֲלִין אֶת הַבִּכּוּרִים. כָּל הָעֲיָרוֹת שֶׁבַּמַּעֲמָד מִתְכַּנְּסוֹת לְעִירוֹ שֶׁל מַעֲמָד כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲלוּ יְחִידִים שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (משלי יד כח) "בְּרָב עָם הַדְרַת מֶלֶךְ". וּבָאִים וְלָנִים בִּרְחוֹבָהּ שֶׁל עִיר. וְלֹא יִכָּנְסוּ לַבָּתִּים מִפְּנֵי אֹהֶל הַטֻּמְאָה. וּבַשַּׁחַר הַמְמֻנֶּה אוֹמֵר (ירמיה לא ה) "קוּמוּ וְנַעֲלֶה צִיּוֹן אֶל ה' אֱלֹהֵינוּ". וְהַשּׁוֹר הוֹלֵךְ לִפְנֵיהֶם וְקַרְנָיו מְצֻפִּין זָהָב וַעֲטָרָה שֶׁל זַיִת בְּרֹאשׁוֹ לְהוֹדִיעַ שֶׁהַבִּכּוּרִים מִשִּׁבְעַת הַמִּינִין. וְהֶחָלִיל מַכֶּה לִפְנֵיהֶם עַד שֶׁהֵם מַגִּיעִין קָרוֹב לִירוּשָׁלַיִם וְהֵם הוֹלְכִין בְּכָל הַדֶּרֶךְ וְקוֹרִין (תהילים קכב א) "שָׂמַחְתִּי בְּאֹמְרִים לִי בֵּית ה' נֵלֵךְ". וְלֹא הָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין בַּיּוֹם אֶלָּא שְׁתֵּי יָדוֹת בִּלְבַד. הִגִּיעוּ קָרוֹב לִירוּשָׁלַיִם שָׁלְחוּ לִפְנֵיהֶם שְׁלוּחִין לְהוֹדִיעַ לְאַנְשֵׁי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם וְעִטְּרוּ אֶת בִּכּוּרֵיהֶם וּפִרְכְּסוּ אוֹתָן. וְאִם הָיָה לָהֶם לַח וְיָבֵשׁ מַרְאִין אֶת הַלַּח מִלְּמַעְלָה. וְהַפַּחוֹת וְהַסְּגָנִים וְהַגִּזְבָּרִים יוֹצְאִין לִקְרָאתָן מִירוּשָׁלַיִם. לְפִי הַבָּאִים הֵם יוֹצְאִין אִם בָּאוּ אֲנָשִׁים הַרְבֵּה יוֹצְאִין לִקְרָאתָן רַבִּים. וְאִם מְעַט מְעַט. וּמִשֶּׁיִּכָּנְסוּ כֻּלָּן בְּשַׁעֲרֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם יַתְחִילוּ לִקְרוֹת (תהילים קכב ב) "עֹמְדוֹת הָיוּ רַגְלֵינוּ בִּשְׁעָרַיִךְ יְרוּשָׁלָיִם":
How are the first fruits brought to Jerusalem? All of [the inhabitants of] the towns in a regional area57See Hilchot K'lei HaMikdash 6:1 for more particulars concerning the Hebrew term. gather in the central town of the regional area, so that they will not ascend to Jerusalem as individuals, for [Proverbs 14:28] teaches: "The glory of the King is among the multitude of people."58Thus coming as a group is a greater act of Divine glorification.They come and sleep in the city's thoroughfare, but they do not enter the homes lest they contract impurity because of being under the same shelter as a corpse.59I.e., the first fruits must be brought in a state of ritual purity, and if a person bringing them enters a home, he may unknowingly contract ritual impurity, because he was under the same shelter as a corpse. 🎺 Glory of the Many
First fruits become the priest's private property — usable like any asset, including to purchase land, servants, or a Torah scroll. Mixed with ordinary produce outside Jerusalem, they are nullified at 1:101; inside Jerusalem, even a trace forbids the mixture, since they could become permitted there. The procession itself embodies national glory: towns converge, an ox with golden horns leads the way, a flute plays, artisans in Jerusalem stand in honor, and Levites sing Psalm 30 as the first fruits arrive at the Courtyard — 'The glory of the King is among the multitude of people.'

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 4
📜
Declaration Requires True Ownership
The verbal declaration 'the land You gave me' is reserved for those with genuine, unambiguous ownership of the land — partial rights, agency, or doubt disqualify the words even when the obligation to bring remains.
🌍
The Covenant Embraces Converts Fully
A convert brings first fruits and makes the full declaration because Abraham — spiritual father to all who enter God's presence — received the oath of the land, making every convert a true heir of that promise.
🤐
Integrity of Moment: Wholeness Enables Declaration
The declaration is an expression of spiritual and material wholeness; any disruption — lost produce, sold land, impurity, wrong season, or broken chain of bringing — silences the words while preserving the act.
🎺
First Fruits Are a National, Joyful Celebration
The procession — towns converging, flutes playing, Levites singing, artisans standing in honor — transforms a personal agricultural offering into a communal act of thanksgiving that glorifies God through the multitude.
