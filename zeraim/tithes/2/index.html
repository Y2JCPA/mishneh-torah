Intent and Ownership: The Foundation of Tithe Obligation

הלכות א׳–ב׳
הלכה א׳
אֵינּוֹ חַיָּב לְהַפְרִישׁ מִן הַתּוֹרָה אֶלָּא הַגּוֹמֵר פֵּרוֹתָיו לְאָכְלָן לְעַצְמוֹ אֲבָל הַגּוֹמְרָן לְמָכְרָן פָּטוּר מִן הַתּוֹרָה וְחַיָּב מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (דברים יד כב) "עַשֵּׂר תְּעַשֵּׂר" וְגוֹ' (דברים יד כג) "וְאָכַלְתָּ" אֵינוֹ חַיָּב אֶלָּא הַגּוֹמֵר תְּבוּאָתוֹ לְאָכְלָהּ:
A person is not obligated to tithe his produce by Scriptural Law unless he completes [the work associated with its preparation1At which point the obligation to tithe takes effect (Chapter 3, Halachah 1). with the intent of] partaking of it himself.2Rabbi Akiva Eiger and the Minchat Chinuch (mitzvah 395) explain that even if the person changes his mind and desires to sell the produce afterwards, he remains obligated to tithe. If, however, he completes those tasks with the intent of selling it, he is exempt according to Scriptural Law.3If the person changes his mind and desires to partake of the produce himself afterwards, he is not obligated according to Scriptural Law. Since he was not obligated at the time of the initial requirement, he does not become obligated afterwards (ibid.). He is, however, obligated by Rabbinic decree.4Our Sages adopted this measure as a safeguard. Ignoring it can have serious repercussions as Bava Metzia 88a states the shopping area of Beit Hino was destroyed three years before Jerusalem because they did not tithe produce that was sold in the marketplace. [This is derived from Deuteronomy 14:22:] "And you shall certainly tithe... and you shall partake...." One is obligated only when he completes the tasks with the intent of partaking.
הלכה ב׳
וְכֵן הַלּוֹקֵחַ פָּטוּר מִן הַתּוֹרָה שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (דברים יד כב) "תְּבוּאַת זַרְעֶךָ". וְחַיָּב מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים בְּשֶׁלְּקָחָן אַחַר שֶׁנִּגְמְרוּ מְלַאכְתָּן בְּיַד מוֹכֵר. אֲבָל אִם נִגְמְרוּ בְּיַד לוֹקֵחַ חַיָּב לְעַשֵּׂר מִן הַתּוֹרָה:
Similarly, one who purchases such produce is exempt according to Scriptural Law, [for ibid. states]: "the produce of your crops."5I.e., crops that you made ready for use. He is, however, obligated according to Rabbinic Law.When does the above apply? When he purchased it after the work necessary to prepare it was completed while it was in the possession of the seller. If, however, these tasks were completed while it was in the possession of the purchaser, he is obligated to tithe according to Scriptural Law.6The Ra'avad differs with the Rambam, maintaining that the purchaser of the produce is never liable to tithe according to Scriptural Law. The Radbaz and the Kessef Mishneh cite interpretations of Talmudic passages that support the Rambam's position, but explain that there are other Rishonim who follow the Ra'avad's position.
🎯 Intent Matters
Scriptural tithe obligation hinges on intent at the moment of completion: preparing produce to eat yourself obligates you by Torah law; preparing it to sell does not — though Rabbinic decree still applies. If the work was completed by the seller, the buyer is only Rabbinically obligated; if the buyer completes the work himself, he is Scripturally obligated.

The Phase of Tithing: Species-by-Species Thresholds

הלכות ג׳–ה׳
הלכה ג׳
פֵּרוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיִים לַאֲכִילָה מִקָּטְנָן כְּגוֹן הַבֹּסֶר וְכַיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ אֵינָן חַיָּבִין בְּמַעֲשֵׂר עַד שֶׁיִּגְדְּלוּ וְיֵעָשׂוּ אֹכֶל שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (ויקרא כז ל) "מִזֶּרַע הָאָרֶץ מִפְּרִי הָעֵץ" עַד שֶׁיִּהְיֶה פְּרִי. וְכֵן הַתְּבוּאָה וְהַקִּטְנִיּוֹת שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (דברים יד כב) "אֵת כָּל תְּבוּאַת זַרְעֶךָ" עַד שֶׁתֵּעָשֶׂה תְּבוּאָה וְזוֹ הִיא עוֹנַת הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת. וְקֹדֶם שֶׁתַּגִּיעַ הַתְּבוּאָה וְהַפֵּרוֹת לְעוֹנָה זוֹ מֻתָּר לֶאֱכל מֵהֶן כָּל מַה שֶּׁיִּרְצֶה וּבְכָל דֶּרֶךְ שֶׁיִּרְצֶה:

הלכה ד׳
פֵּרוֹת שֶׁהֵן רְאוּיִין לַאֲכִילָה בְּקָטְנָן כְּגוֹן הַקִּשּׁוּאִים וְהַמְּלָפְפוֹנוֹת שֶׁאֵין מַנִּיחִין אוֹתָן אֶלָּא כְּדֵי לְהוֹסִיף בְּגוּפָן בִּלְבַד אֲבָל רְאוּיִין הֵן מִתְּחִלָּה לַאֲכִילָה. הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּבִין בְּמַעֲשֵׂר בְּקָטְנָן. שֶׁמִּתְּחִלַּת יְצִיאָתָן בָּאוּ לְעוֹנַת הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת:
Produce that is fit to be eaten when it is small, e.g., zucchini or cucumbers, but is left [to grow] only in order to increase its bulk, is required to be tithed while it is small, [for] they are fit to be eaten at the outset. As soon as they emerge, they are considered to have reached the "phase of tithing."

🌱 Produce Must Mature
Before produce reaches the "phase of tithing" — approximately one-third of its natural growth — there is no obligation at all, not even Rabbinically. The phase varies by species: figs soften, grapes show seeds through the skin, olives yield a ninth of their oil. Produce fit to eat while small (like cucumbers) enters its phase immediately upon emergence. Once even one grape on a cluster reaches this phase, the entire cluster and that side of the vine becomes obligated.

הלכות ו׳–ח׳
הלכה ו׳
לֹא יִמְכֹּר אָדָם פֵּרוֹתָיו מִשֶּׁבָּאוּ לְעוֹנַת הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת לְמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן עַל הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת. וְאִם בִּכְּרוּ נוֹטֵל אֶת הַבִּכּוּרוֹת וּמֻתָּר לִמְכֹּר הַשְּׁאָר שֶׁעֲדַיִן לֹא הִגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת. וְכֵן לֹא יִמְכֹּר אָדָם אֶת גִּפְתּוֹ וְאֶת זַגָּיו לְמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן עַל הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת לְהוֹצִיא מֵהֶן מַשְׁקִין. שֶׁהַמַּשְׁקִין הַיּוֹצְאִין מֵהֶם חַיָּבִין בְּמַעַשְׂרוֹת. וְלֹא אֶת תִּבְנוֹ לִלְקֹט מִמֶּנּוּ תְּבוּאָה לְמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן שֶׁהַתְּבוּאָה שֶׁתִּלָּקֵט מִן הַתֶּבֶן חַיֶּבֶת בְּמַעֲשֵׂר:
After produce has reached the "phase of tithing," a person should not sell it to someone who is not trusted with regard to tithes.26Lest that person fail to tithe it and the seller thus be liable for "placing a stumbling block in the path of the blind." If [a portion of] the produce ripened,27I.e., reached a stage when it was required to separate the tithes. he should remove that [portion] which ripened. It is then permitted to sell the remainder that has not reached the "phase of tithing."28The Radbaz notes that this clause appears to be in contradiction with the final clause of the previous halachah, for seemingly if part of the crop has become obligated in the tithes, the entire crop has. He explains that if the grapes are growing on different vines, the stringency mentioned in the previous halachah does not apply.Similarly, a person should not sell olive dregs and grape dregs to someone who is not trusted with regard to tithes to extract [oil or wine] from them,29I.e., the purchaser stated that he was purchasing them for this purpose. If, however, such a statement of intent is not made, these may be sold, for it is most likely they will be used as fuel for a fire. for the liquids extracted from these dregs must be tithed. Nor should he sell straw so that grain can be picked from it to someone who is not trusted, for the grain picked from the straw must be tithed.
הלכה ז׳
שִׁמְרֵי יַיִן שֶׁנָּתַן עֲלֵיהֶם מַיִם וְסִנְּנָן אִם נָתַן שְׁלֹשָׁה וּמָצָא אַרְבָּעָה מוֹצִיא מַעֲשֵׂר מִזֶּה הַיֶּתֶר מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר. וְאֵינוֹ מַפְרִישׁ עָלָיו תְּרוּמָה שֶׁהַתּוֹרֵם בַּתְּחִלָּה בְּלִבּוֹ עַל הַכּל כְּמוֹ שֶׁבֵּאַרְנוּ בִּתְרוּמָה. מָצָא פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבָּעָה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמָּצָא יֶתֶר עַל מִדָּתוֹ וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן טַעַם יַיִן פָּטוּר:
[The following laws apply when a person] mixed water with wine dregs and filtered the mixture. If he mixed in three measures of water and received four measures, [the mixture is considered wine] and he must separate tithes for this extra measure from another source.30The bracketed addition is made on the basis of the gloss of Rabbi Ovadiah of Bartenura to Ma'aserot 5:6. For there is no reason why that produce itself cannot be used for the tithes. He does not have to separate terumah, for when one separates terumah, he has the intent to separate terumah for the entire amount, as stated above with regard to terumah.31Hilchot Terumah 4:21. The Radbaz explains that this proviso is made with regard to terumah and not with regard to the tithes, because terumah is given by estimation, while the tithes must be measured exactly. If he received less than four measures, he is exempt, even if he received more than he mixed in and even if the mixture has the flavor of wine.32As long as the wine is not one fourth of the new mixture, it is not considered as significant.
הלכה ח׳
הַמַּקְדִּישׁ פֵּרוֹתָיו כְּשֶׁהֵן מְחֻבָּרִין עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ לְעוֹנַת הַמַּעֲשֵׂר וּפְדָאָן וְאַחַר שֶׁפְּדָאָן הִגִּיעוּ חַיָּבִין בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. וְאִם בָּאוּ לְעוֹנַת הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת וְהֵם בְּיַד הַגִּזְבָּר וְאַחַר כָּךְ פְּדָאָן פְּטוּרִין:
When a person consecrated his produce when it was attached to the ground, before they reached the "phase of tithing," and then redeemed them, and after he redeemed them, it reached that stage of development, he is obligated to tithe them.33Because at the time the obligation to tithe them took effect, the crops were ordinary produce. If the produce reached the "phase of tithing" while it was in the domain of the Temple treasurer and he redeemed it afterwards, he is exempt.34Because at the time the obligation to tithe them took effect, the crops were consecrated property and there is no obligation to tithe consecrated property.
⚖️ Edge Cases
Once produce reaches its tithe phase, one must not sell it to someone untrustworthy in tithe observance — doing so violates "placing a stumbling block." Wine dregs diluted with water require tithing only if the yield exceeds the input by at least a quarter. For consecrated produce, the moment of obligation is decisive: if the phase of tithing arrived while the produce was consecrated, redemption afterwards does not create an obligation — but if it was redeemed first, then matured, it is fully obligated.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 2
🎯
Intent Activates Scriptural Obligation
The Torah's tithe requirement only applies when a person completes the preparation of produce with the explicit intent to eat it himself — selling-intent at that moment exempts him by Scriptural Law, though Rabbinic decree still covers the gap.
🌱
The Phase of Tithing Is Species-Specific
Each species has a precise biological threshold — roughly one-third growth — after which tithing becomes obligatory, and Rambam enumerates unique signs for dozens of fruits, grains, and vegetables.
🤝
Communal Responsibility Extends to Sales
Once produce is obligated, selling it to someone known to neglect tithes is forbidden, as the seller becomes complicit in the buyer's violation of Torah law.
⏱️
The Moment of Obligation Is Fixed
Whether produce is consecrated, purchased, or mixed into a beverage, the decisive factor is always the status of the produce at the precise moment the tithe obligation first takes effect.
