Rav Amram Gaon records that in Babylonia, it was the custom to recite such prayers every night except Friday.Even though one recites the blessing hashkiveinu7the second blessing recited after the Shema at night afterga'al Yisrael, it is not considered as an interruption between [the blessing of] redemption and prayer, for they are both considered as one long blessing.8Hashkiveinu is considered as a continuation of the theme of redemption (Tosafot, Berachot 4b) because it contains the request for God to save us from many different types of difficulties just as He redeemed us from Egypt (Maharit Algazi). The connection is further emphasized by the fact that in the exodus from Egypt, God protected the Jews on a night where permission was granted for the forces of destruction to act. Similarly, in hashkiveinu, we ask Him to protect us from "all evil matters and fears of night" (Birkai Yosef).
