📜 Biblical Foundation
The four commemorative fasts — Third of Tishrei, Tenth of Tevet, Seventeenth of Tammuz, and Ninth of Av — are rooted in Zechariah 8:19 and obligatory for all Jews after the Temple's destruction (Halachot 1–4). Tisha B'Av alone witnessed five distinct national catastrophes, including the decree against the desert generation and the destruction of both Temples. A fifth fast, the Thirteenth of Adar, is observed by universal custom in memory of the Fast of Esther (Halacha 5).

🌑 Darkening Days
From Rosh Chodesh Av, joy is reduced: bathing, laundry, haircuts, meat, and wine are restricted; during the week of Tisha B'Av, leather shoes are also forbidden (Halacha 6). Tisha B'Av restrictions begin at sunset the prior evening, and the pre-fast meal eaten in the afternoon must be somber — only bread, salt, and water (Halachot 7–8). The pious of earlier generations ate this meal alone by the stove, weeping as though their dead lay before them, and Torah scholars are urged to follow this practice (Halacha 9).

⚫ Total Affliction
Unlike other fasts, pregnant and nursing women must complete the Tisha B'Av fast in full (Halacha 10). The day carries five prohibitions: eating and drinking, washing, anointing, wearing leather shoes, and marital relations — even placing a finger in water is forbidden. Upon seeing destroyed cities of Judah, Jerusalem, or the Temple Mount, one recites specific scriptural verses and rends one's garments by hand — never with a utensil — with a separate tear required for each holy site (Halachot 16–17). 🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 5
📜
Prophecy Anchors Obligation
The four commemorative fasts derive their obligatory force from Zechariah 8:19, making them binding for all Jews after the destruction of the Temple.
🌑
Grief Is Graduated and Cumulative
Mourning intensifies in stages — from a month-wide reduction of joy at Rosh Chodesh Av, to near-total affliction on Tisha B'Av itself, mirroring the depth of national loss.
⚒️
Rabbinic Ordinances Embed Loss Into Daily Life
Post-Temple decrees permanently alter how Jews build homes, set tables, wear jewelry, listen to music, and celebrate weddings, so that mourning is never merely seasonal.
✨
Fasting Points Beyond Itself Toward Redemption
All commemorative fasts are inherently temporary — in the Messianic era they will be abolished and converted into days of celebration, revealing that their true purpose was always return and repair.
