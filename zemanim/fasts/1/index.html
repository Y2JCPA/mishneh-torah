📯 Torah Commandment to Cry Out
A positive Torah commandment (halacha 1) obligates us to cry out and sound trumpets whenever communal distress strikes — famine, plague, locusts, or war. Halacha 2 explains this as a path of repentance: the trumpets jolt people into recognizing sin as the cause of their suffering. Halacha 3 warns that attributing hardship to natural coincidence is a "cruel conception" that perpetuates sin and invites greater Divine punishment, as Leviticus 26 implies.

🤰 Exemptions and the Sleep Cutoff

😔 Mourning Demeanor Required
Halacha 12 uniquely permits — indeed requires — fasting on Shabbat after a disturbing dream without prior acceptance, though one must then fast a compensatory day for neglecting Sabbath pleasure. Halacha 13 validates an hourly fast for remaining daylight hours when a same-day commitment is made. Halacha 14 mandates a somber, mourning-like demeanor during any fast, allowing only tasting food (up to a revi'it) without swallowing; halacha 15 rules that an individual who began fasting must complete the fast even if the distress resolves, and a traveler joins the stringencies of both his origin and destination.

🌧️ Rain Before Noon = Celebration
Halacha 16 rules that if rain falls before noon on a communal rain-fast, the community stops fasting, eats, and recites the Great Hallel (Psalm 136) in gratitude; rain after noon means completing the fast since "the majority of the day passed in holiness." Halacha 17 prescribes the court and elders' communal program: morning review of the city's conduct and removal of moral stumbling blocks, afternoon reading of Torah curses, and a final quarter of afternoon prayer, supplications, and public confession.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 1
📯
Cry Out, Don't Rationalize
The Torah forbids attributing communal hardship to coincidence — silence in the face of distress locks a community into its sins and guarantees worse punishment.
🗓️
Sacred Time Is Shielded from Decree
Sabbath, Yom Tov, Rosh Chodesh, Chanukah, and Purim cannot have new communal fasts imposed upon them, though an already-running series of fasts may continue through them.
🌙
The Afternoon Before Binds the Day After
An individual's fast has no halachic standing unless formally accepted with words in the Mincha prayer before the previous sunset — even eating all night does not cancel a properly accepted fast.
⚖️
Community and Individual Mirror Each Other
Just as courts obligate the community to fast through shared distress, so too each individual bears a personal obligation to fast for those close to him who are sick, lost, or imprisoned.
