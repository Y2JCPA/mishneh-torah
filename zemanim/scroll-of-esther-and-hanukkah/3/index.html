The Chanukah Miracle: History and Commemoration

הלכה א׳
בְבַיִת שֵׁנִי כְּשֶׁמַּלְכֵי יָוָן גָּזְרוּ גְּזֵרוֹת עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל וּבִטְּלוּ דָּתָם וְלֹא הֵנִיחוּ אוֹתָם לַעֲסֹק בְּתוֹרָה וּבְמִצְוֹת. וּפָשְׁטוּ יָדָם בְּמָמוֹנָם וּבִבְנוֹתֵיהֶם וְנִכְנְסוּ לַהֵיכָל וּפָרְצוּ בּוֹ פְּרָצוֹת וְטִמְּאוּ הַטָּהֳרוֹת. וְצָר לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְאֹד מִפְּנֵיהֶם וּלְחָצוּם לַחַץ גָּדוֹל עַד שֶׁרִחֵם עֲלֵיהֶם אֱלֹהֵי אֲבוֹתֵינוּ וְהוֹשִׁיעָם מִיָּדָם וְהִצִּילָם וְגָבְרוּ בְּנֵי חַשְׁמוֹנַאי הַכֹּהֲנִים הַגְּדוֹלִים וַהֲרָגוּם וְהוֹשִׁיעוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִיָּדָם וְהֶעֱמִידוּ מֶלֶךְ מִן הַכֹּהֲנִים וְחָזְרָה מַלְכוּת לְיִשְׂרָאֵל יֶתֶר עַל מָאתַיִם שָׁנָה עַד הַחֻרְבָּן הַשֵּׁנִי:

🕎 The Founding Story
The Greek kingdom defiled the Temple and issued decrees against Torah observance until the Hasmonean priests overthrew them and entered the Sanctuary on the twenty-fifth of Kislev. A single cruse of pure oil, sufficient for only one day, burned miraculously for eight days until new oil could be produced. The Sages therefore ordained eight days of joy, praise, and candle-lighting beginning on the twenty-fifth of Kislev — forbidden to eulogize or fast, equal in rabbinic status to the reading of the Megillah.

🕯️ Obligation and Blessing
Whoever is obligated to read the Megillah — men and women alike — is obligated to kindle the Chanukah lamp, since women were also included in the miracle. On the first night, three blessings are recited (l'hadlik, she'asah nissim, shehecheyanu); on subsequent nights, only two; a person who merely sees a lamp recites the latter two on the first night, one thereafter. The complete Hallel is recited every one of the eight days with a blessing, whether praying individually or communally, because Hallel is a definite Rabbinic obligation over which a blessing is always recited.

📅 Eighteen Days of Full Hallel
The obligation to recite Hallel at all times is entirely Rabbinic — not Scriptural — including on the festivals themselves. In Eretz Yisrael, the complete Hallel is recited on eighteen days: eight days of Sukkot, eight of Chanukah, the first day of Pesach, and Shavuot; in the diaspora, twenty-one. Rosh HaShanah and Yom Kippur have no Hallel because they are days of awe, not extra celebration; Purim has no Hallel because the reading of the Megillah serves that purpose. Rosh Chodesh Hallel is merely a custom, recited communally with passages skipped and without a blessing.

⏱️ Rules of Recitation
On Rosh Chodesh and the latter days of Pesach, specific passages are skipped: one reads through Psalm 114, then jumps to Psalm 115:12, then Psalm 116:12, then Psalm 118:5 to the end. Hallel recited out of order does not fulfill one's obligation, though pausing at length between sections does not invalidate it. On days when complete Hallel is said (a full Rabbinic commandment), one may not interrupt within a chapter but may interrupt between chapters; on partial-Hallel days the rules are more lenient even within a chapter.

🗣️ The Living Tradition of Hallel
A concluding blessing is recited after Hallel on all days when the complete Hallel is said; in places where it is customary to do so, the verses from od'cha ki anitani onward are repeated, yielding a total congregation response of 123 Hallelujahs throughout the entire Hallel — a mnemonic for the years of Aaron's life. In the era of the early Sages, the reader led verse by verse and the congregation responded 'Halleluyah' after each bar, and at the opening of each psalm the congregation repeated the first line. If the leader was a minor, slave, or woman — none of whom are obligated — adults must repeat every word themselves to fulfill their obligation.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 3
🕎
Pirsumei Nisa — Publicizing the Miracle
Chanukah candles are lit at night at the entrance of the home specifically to project their light into the public domain and proclaim the miracle.
📜
All Hallel Is Rabbinic
The obligation to recite Hallel — even on Biblical festivals — is entirely a Rabbinic ordinance, not a Torah commandment.
⚖️
Definite Obligation Requires a Blessing
A blessing is recited over a Rabbinic mitzvah only when the obligation is definite; a doubtful obligation (such as tithing d'mai) does not warrant a blessing.
🔄
Listener as Reciter — With Limits
One who listens to Hallel is considered to have recited it, but only when the leader is themselves obligated; if led by a minor, slave, or woman, adults must repeat every word themselves.
