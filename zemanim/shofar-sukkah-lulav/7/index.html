🌿 The Four Species
Palm fronds are young date-palm branches whose leaves have not yet separated — the lulav. The fruit of a beautiful tree is the etrog. A branch of a thick-leaved tree is the myrtle (hadas) whose leaves cover the stem. Willows of the brook is a known species with elongated leaves, smooth edges, and red wood — not the tzaftzafah which resembles it but is disqualified for its round leaves and serrated edge.

⚖️ Measures and the Ideal Practice
The four species are one mitzvah and are interdependent; one may not add or subtract. It is ideal to bind lulav, myrtle, and willow together. The quantities: one lulav, one etrog, two willow branches and three myrtle branches. Minimum lengths: lulav no less than four handbreadths (measured from its spine only); myrtle and willow no less than three handbreadths. The waving: extend and shake the lulav tip three times, draw back and shake, likewise upward and downward — during Hallel.

📅 Rabbinic Enactments Across the Generations
By Torah law, the lulav is required only on the first day of Sukkot, everywhere and at all times. During Temple times the Sages limited the provinces to one day; after the destruction, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai enacted that the lulav be taken all seven days as a memorial to the Temple. On Shabbat — in Temple times it was taken on the first day even on Shabbat; after the destruction the Sages forbade it, and in our time the lulav is never taken on Shabbat — neither in the provinces nor in Eretz Yisrael. The obligation parallels shofar and sukkah: women and slaves are exempt; a minor who knows how to wave is obligated by Rabbinic law.

🏛️ Temple Service and Muktzeh Restrictions
It is a halacha from Moses at Sinai that a separate willow is brought in the Temple apart from the willow in the lulav, and it does not fulfill the lulav's willow requirement. Large willow branches were stood alongside the altar all seven days; on Shabbat they were prepared in golden vessels from the day before. Each day the altar was circled once; on the seventh day, seven times. Jerusalem custom: to hold the lulav in hand all day. It is forbidden to smell the myrtle in the lulav since it is designated for the mitzvah. The etrog may not be eaten on the seventh day — designated for part of the day means designated for all of it; on the eighth day it may be eaten.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 7
🌿
Four Species — One Mitzvah
Lulav, etrog, myrtle, and willow are interdependent and combine into one mitzvah. The absence of any one species invalidates them all.
⚖️
Precision in Measures and Quantities
The Torah specified the species; the Sages detailed minimum sizes. Lulav — 4 handbreadths; myrtle and willow — 3 handbreadths; two willow branches and three myrtle branches required.
📅
Halachic Adaptation Across Generations
The lulav obligation underwent transformations: a provincial enactment, partial suspension, and Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's institution of seven days as a Temple memorial — a prime example of preserving a mitzvah through adaptation.
🏛️
Muktzeh for a Mitzvah
An object designated for a mitzvah is forbidden for mundane use while the mitzvah is in effect. Therefore it is forbidden to smell the myrtle in the lulav and forbidden to eat the etrog on the seventh day.
