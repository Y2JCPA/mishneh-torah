הלכות חמץ ומצה פרק ד · 5 Questions
Question 1
You know there's chametz buried under rubble in your yard during Pesach. Are you in violation?
Knowing about your chametz and owning it is enough. Physical inaccessibility doesn't remove ownership or knowledge — both prohibitions are violated.
Question 2
A non-Jewish friend stores their bread in your house during Pesach. Is this a problem?
Bal yeira'eh and bal yimatzei target YOUR chametz. A non-Jew's chametz stored in your space: no violation, because it's not yours.
Question 3
What happens to chametz that a Jew owned throughout Pesach (and didn't destroy)?
Chametz she'avar alav haPesach: chametz owned by a Jew during Pesach is forever forbidden as a penalty. You can't eat it, sell it, or benefit from it — even after Pesach ends.
Question 4
If you properly nullified (bitul) your chametz before Pesach, and then find some during Pesach, are you in violation?
Effective bitul before Pesach removes the ownership. The chametz is legally not yours — finding it doesn't create a new violation (though you should still destroy it).
Question 5
How many Torah prohibitions does owning chametz during Pesach violate?
Two separate prohibitions: "no chametz shall be SEEN for you" and "no leaven shall be FOUND in your houses." The Torah uses two phrases to create two distinct violations for the same act.
