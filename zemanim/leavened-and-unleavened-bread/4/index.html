📜 Sources and Scope
The Rambam derives from three verses the full scope: 'lo yera'eh' (Exodus 13:7), 'lo yimatzei' (Exodus 12:19), and 'in all your borders.' A Jew's chametz is forbidden wherever it is — hidden, in another city, or deposited with a gentile. By contrast, chametz belonging to a gentile or to hekdesh found in a Jew's domain is permitted, since it is not his. A gentile's chametz requires a partition ten handbreadths high to prevent accidental use, but hekdesh chametz needs no partition — everyone avoids it for fear of me'ilah.

הלכה ה׳
יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהִרְהִין חֲמֵצוֹ אֵצֶל הָעַכּוּ"ם אִם אָמַר לוֹ אִם לֹא הֵבֵאתִי לְךָ מָעוֹת מִכָּאן וְעַד יוֹם פְּלוֹנִי קְנֵה חָמֵץ זֶה מֵעַכְשָׁו הֲרֵי זֶה בִּרְשׁוּת הָעַכּוּ"ם וְאוֹתוֹ הֶחָמֵץ מֻתָּר לְאַחַר הַפֶּסַח. וְהוּא שֶׁיִּהְיֶה אוֹתוֹ זְמַן שֶׁקָּבַע לוֹ קֹדֶם הַפֶּסַח. וְאִם לֹא אָמַר לוֹ קְנֵה מֵעַכְשָׁו נִמְצָא אוֹתוֹ הֶחָמֵץ כְּאִלּוּ הוּא פִּקָּדוֹן אֵצֶל הָעַכּוּ"ם וְאָסוּר בַּהֲנָיָה לְאַחַר הַפֶּסַח:

The Ra'avad maintains that, generally, the fact that a borrower does not specify that the security would retroactively become the lender's property implies that he never really intended to sell it to him and always considers it as his own. Thus, were such a transaction to be carried out between Jews, the Ra'avad maintains that the security would never become the lender's property. However, he explains that this law applies only regarding business dealings carried out between Jews, and not to those involving gentiles. Therefore, in this instance, the chametz given as security becomes the gentile's property.In contrast, the Rambam does not consider such an agreement an Asmachtah. In the former instance, a Jew is considered the owner of the chametz if the agreement included the clause specifying retroactive ownership, even though the time for payment is not fixed until after Pesach. If the agreement lacked that clause, and the Jew is not held responsible for the chametz (see Halachah 3 above), the Jew is not liable for that chametz (Shulchan Aruch). Nevertheless, other authorities do not accept this decision.
⚖️ Responsibility = Ownership
A gentile who deposited chametz with a Jew: if the Jew accepted responsibility to pay if lost or stolen — he must destroy it, since it is considered his. Without responsibility — he may keep it and eat it after Pesach. Even a coercive gentile who forces the Jew to pay — though no explicit responsibility was accepted — the Jew must destroy it. A Jew who pledged his chametz to a gentile: if he said 'acquire it from now' with a date before Pesach — the chametz belongs to the gentile and is permitted after Pesach; without 'acquire from now' — it is considered a deposit and forbidden for benefit after Pesach.

A chametz mixture fit for eating — one violates bal yera'eh and bal yimatzei (fish brine, Babylonian kotach, Median beer). A mixture unfit for eating — may be kept. Salves, bandages, plasters, and antidotes — may be kept. Bread that has molded beyond dog food quality, garments washed in wheat milk, papers glued with chametz — may be kept. Any non-food substance containing chametz — may be kept but may not be eaten until after Pesach, even in the smallest quantity.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 4
📜
Legal Ownership, Not Physical Location
The prohibitions of bal yera'eh and bal yimatzei depend on legal ownership of the chametz, not its physical location — even chametz hidden, in another city, or deposited with a gentile is forbidden.
⚖️
Financial Responsibility Creates Ownership
Accepting responsibility to pay for chametz (whether voluntarily or by coercion from a gentile) transforms the chametz into the Jew's property for halachic purposes, requiring destruction.
🤝
Transfer of Ownership Must Be Absolute
Sale or gift of chametz to a gentile is valid only if complete and unconditional. A conditional sale is ineffective — the Jew remains the owner and violates the prohibition.
🧪
The Chametz's Form Determines Its Status
Chametz that has lost its food form (spoiled, used for tanning, mixed into medicines) is no longer treated as chametz for possession purposes. But if a mixture is still edible, even a tiny amount triggers the prohibition.
