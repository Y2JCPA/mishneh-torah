🕍 The Opening Structure
The Seder opens by pouring a cup for each person and reciting kiddush with the shehecheyanu blessing. Then one recites borei pri ha'adamah, dips a vegetable in charoset, and eats at least a k'zayit — as do all participants. After breaking the matzah and beginning the Haggadah, in our time we do not say 'this night is entirely roasted' since there is no sacrifice. One begins with disgrace and recites 'Arami oved avi' through its conclusion.

🫓 The Eating Obligations
In our time (without the sacrifice): recite hamotzi, then the blessing for eating matzah — dip in charoset and eat. Recite the blessing for eating maror — dip and eat. Then continue the meal. At the meal's end, eat a k'zayit of matzah (afikoman) and taste nothing afterward. Drink the third cup over birkat hamazon, pour the fourth cup, and complete Hallel with birkat hashir.

⚖️ Situational Laws
Roasted meat: where the custom is to eat it — permitted; where not — forbidden, as a decree lest people say it is the Pesach sacrifice. No wine: recite kiddush over bread as on Shabbat. No vegetable except maror — split it: half for karpas, half for maror. Only one k'zayit of guarded matzah: finish the meal with regular matzah, then recite the blessing and eat the guarded k'zayit last. Falling asleep during the meal: an individual who slept — does not eat again; some of the group fell asleep — they resume; the entire group fell asleep — they may not eat again.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 8
🍷
Four Cups Frame the Seder
The four cups mark the Seder's four stages: kiddush (cup 1), end of Haggadah (cup 2), birkat hamazon (cup 3), and completion of Hallel (cup 4). Each requires its own blessing.
📖
Maggid: From Disgrace to Praise
The obligation is to begin with disgrace — 'Originally our ancestors were idol worshipers' — and conclude with praise and thanksgiving, following the principle of 'begin with disgrace and end with praise.'
🫓
Afikoman — The Final Taste
The matzah (in our time) or the Pesach sacrifice (in Temple times) is the last thing eaten at the Seder. Nothing may be tasted afterward, so its flavor lingers in the mouth.
🏠
Local Custom Is Decisive
Whether roasted meat may be eaten at the Seder depends entirely on local custom — reflecting the principle that 'the custom of their ancestors is in their hands.'
