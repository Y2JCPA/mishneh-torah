📖 Tell Your Child
It is a positive Torah commandment to recount the miracles and wonders of the Exodus on the night of the 15th of Nisan. The obligation falls on everyone — even when the child does not ask. A father must teach according to the child's understanding: simply for a young child, with halachah and midrash for an older one. One should make changes on this night so that children see and ask the Mah Nishtanah question.

🕍 From Disgrace to Praise

🛋️ The Way of Freedom
On this night one must eat and drink in the manner of freedom. Even the poorest Jew — with no couch or cushion — must recline, and must sell his garment if necessary to buy wine for four cups. The four cups are diluted with water so the taste is pleasant for drinking. Each cup represents a stage of the redemption.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 7
📖
Telling Is a Torah Obligation
The mitzvah of telling the Exodus story is a Torah obligation — one must speak it aloud, not merely think about it. The more one elaborates, the more praiseworthy.
🕍
Three Things Cannot Be Omitted
Pesach, matzah, and maror — whoever fails to mention all three and explain their reasons has not fulfilled the Haggadah obligation, even if he told many other things.
🛋️
Freedom for Everyone, Without Exception
Reclining, the cups, and the feast are not only for the wealthy. Even the most destitute person must recline in a feast of freedom and sell his garment if needed for wine.
🌿
Maror: From Torah to Rabbinic
Eating maror was a Torah obligation when the Temple stood. Today, without the Pesach sacrifice, the obligation is Rabbinic — a remembrance of the bitterness of slavery.
