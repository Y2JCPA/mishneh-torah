The Mitzvah to Guard: Positive Command and Negative Prohibition

הלכות א׳–ג׳
הלכה א׳
שְׁמִירַת הַמִּקְדָּשׁ מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין שָׁם פַּחַד מֵאוֹיְבִים וְלֹא מִלִּסְטִים. שֶׁאֵין שְׁמִירָתוֹ אֶלָּא כָּבוֹד לוֹ. אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה פַּלְטֵרִין שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו שׁוֹמְרִין לְפַלְטֵרִין שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו שׁוֹמְרִין:
There is a positive mitzvah to guard the Temple.1The Rambam (Sefer HaMitzvot, positive commandment 22) and the Sefer HaChinuch (mitzvah 388) list this as one of the 613 commandments. [This mitzvah applies] even though there is no fear of enemies or thieves, for the guarding [of the Temple] is an expression of respect for it. A palace with guards is [much more impressive] than a palace without guards.2The Kessef Mishneh mentions another reason for guarding the Temple: to prevent those who are forbidden to enter from entering.The place where both the Mishnah and the Rambam describe the watch which the priests and the Levites kept over the Temple has aroused the attention of the commentaries: The Mishnah repeats its description of the watch in two places: at the beginning of the tractate of Tamid, which relates the order of the priests' service, and the beginning of the tractate of Middot, which defines the structure of the Temple. The Rambam describes this mitzvah in Hilchot Beit HaBechirah, which describes the construction of the Temple, but not the details of the priests' service.On this basis, the commentaries explain, we can understand two dimensions implicit in this mitzvah:a) The priests' responsibility to prevent those forbidden to enter from entering; andb) The responsibility incumbent on the entire Jewish people to construct and maintain the Temple in the most fitting manner.Since the presence of guards enhances the glory of the Sanctuary, the mitzvah of guarding the Sanctuary can be seen as an aspect of its very structure. See Likkutei Sichot, Vol. 13, p. 56-65.
הלכה ב׳
וּשְׁמִירָה זוֹ מִצְוָתָהּ כָּל הַלַּיְלָה. וְהַשּׁוֹמְרִים הֵם הַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַלְוִיִּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (במדבר יח ב) "וְאַתָּה וּבָנֶיךָ אִתָּךְ לִפְנֵי אֹהֶל הָעֵדֻת". כְּלוֹמַר אַתֶּם תִּהְיוּ שׁוֹמְרִים לוֹ. וַהֲרֵי נֶאֱמַר (במדבר יח ד) "וְשָׁמְרוּ אֶת מִשְׁמֶרֶת אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד" וְנֶאֱמַר (במדבר ג לח) "וְהַחֹנִים קֵדְמָה לִפְנֵי אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד מִזְרָחָה משֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו שֹׁמְרֵי מִשְׁמֶרֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ":
The mitzvah of guarding [the Temple] applies throughout the night.3In his commentary to Tamid, the Ra'avad states that the priests and Levites also stood watch during the day. The Minchat Chinuch mentions that view, explaining that the Rambam explained that the reason for the priestly watch was to show deference to the Temple during the day as well. Just as a palace of an earthly king is guarded both at day and at night, so too, it is proper to keep watch over the Temple in this manner.Nevertheless, the Tifferet Yisrael (Middot 1:1) explains that since the priests were involved in the Temple service throughout the entire day, that in itself is a clear sign of honor and respect to the Temple, and no further measures were necessary.This view is supported by the commentary of the Rabbenu Asher at the beginning of the tractate of Tamid. He explains that the reason for keeping watch over the Temple was to have our attention focused on it at all times. That purpose is surely accomplished by the Temple service, and hence, watchmen are not required then.Accordingly, it appears that the watch would be maintained until the service in the Temple began, with the removal of the Altar's ashes at dawn. Thus, we can understand the last halachah of this chapter, which deals with the preparation of the Sanctuary for the service of the following day, as describing the final aspects of the nightly watch. See Likkutei Sichot, loc. cit.The priests and Levites shall serve as guards,4The places where the priests and Levites kept watch are described in Halachot 4-9. as [Numbers 18:2] states: "And you and your sons [shall be] before the tent of the testimony,"5This verse begins: "And the Lord said to Aaron," thus, indicating the responsibility of Aaron and his sons, the priests, to keep watch over the Sanctuary. i.e., you shall keep watch over it. Also, [ibid. 18:4] states: "And they [the Levites] shall watch over the Tent of Meeting,"6Indicating that the Levites were also charged with that responsibility. and [ibid. 3:38] states: "And Moses,7A Levite. Aharon, and his sons8Priests. shall camp before the Sanctuary towards the east, and they will be the guardians of the holy watch."9Thus, this verse indicates that the task was shared by the Priests and Levites together (Tamid 26a).It must be noted that, in order to emphasize the concept he wishes to communicate, the Rambam does not quote the latter verse exactly.
הלכה ג׳
וְאִם בִּטְּלוּ שְׁמִירָה עָבְרוּ בְּלֹא תַּעֲשֶׂה שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (במדבר יח ה) "וְשָׁמְרוּ אֶת מִשְׁמֶרֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ". וּלְשׁוֹן שְׁמִירָה אַזְהָרָה הִיא הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁשְּׁמִירָתוֹ מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה. וּבִטּוּל שְׁמִירָתוֹ מִצְוַת לֹא תַּעֲשֶׂה:
Anyone who nullifies this watch transgress a negative commandment,10(Sefer HaMitzvot (negative commandment 67) and the Sefer HaChinuch (mitzvah 391) list this as one of the 613 commandments. as [Numbers 18:5] states: "And you shall take care to keep the Holy Watch." Any form of the verb, shemar, "take care," implies a warning against transgressing a negative command.11See Menachot 36b.Thus, guarding [the Temple fulfills] a positive command and nullifying its watch [represents the violation of] a negative command.
🏛️ Dual Obligation
Guarding the Temple is not merely a security measure — it is a positive mitzvah expressing reverence, like guards stationed before a king's palace. Neglecting the watch violates a negative commandment. Priests and Levites are both charged with this nightly duty by explicit Torah verse.

🕍 Station Map
Twenty-four guard posts operated every night: priests in 3 inner positions (Chamber of Avtinas, Chamber of the Spark, Chamber of the Hearth), and Levites in 21 outer positions. The elders of that day's priestly watch slept in the domed Chamber of the Hearth — the Temple Courtyard keys in their hands.

⚗️ Ritual Purity
Priests on watch slept in their own clothes on the ground — never in priestly garments, never on beds. If a priest became ritually impure through a seminal emission, he descended via a hidden underground passage to immerse, then returned to wait among his brethren until dawn, maintaining the integrity of the watch without disruption.

The Levite Patrol: Eighteen Stations and Three Additional Posts

הלכה ח׳

🚪 21 Positions
The Levites maintained 18 regular stations: inside the five Temple Mount gates, at its four inner corners, outside four corners of the Courtyard, and outside five Courtyard gates. Three additional stations — the Chamber of the Sacrifices, the Chamber of the Parochet, and behind the Holy of Holies — completed their vigil of 21 posts total.

The Overseer's Rounds and the Pre-Dawn Inspection

הלכות י׳–י״ב
הלכה י׳
וּמַעֲמִידִין מְמֻנֶּה אֶחָד עַל כָּל מִשְׁמְרוֹת הַשּׁוֹמְרִים. וְאִישׁ הַר הַבַּיִת הָיָה נִקְרָא. וְהָיָה מְחַזֵּר עַל כָּל מִשְׁמָר וּמִשְׁמָר כָּל הַלַּיְלָה. וַאֲבוּקוֹת דְּלוּקוֹת לְפָנָיו. וְכָל מִשְׁמָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹמֵד וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ אִישׁ הַר הַבַּיִת שָׁלוֹם עָלֶיךָ נִכָּר שֶׁהוּא יָשֵׁן חוֹבְטוֹ בְּמַקְּלוֹ. וּרְשׁוּת הָיָה לוֹ לִשְׂרֹף אֶת כְּסוּתוֹ עַד שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִין בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם מַה קּוֹל בָּעֲזָרָה קוֹל בֶּן לֵוִי לוֹקֶה וּבְגָדָיו נִשְׂרָפִין שֶׁיָּשַׁן עַל מִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ:

🌅 Night to Dawn
The Officer of the Temple Mount patrolled all night by torchlight — a sleeping guard could have his garments burned as punishment. At dawn, the Sanctuary overseer tapped on the Chamber of the Hearth gate; two groups of priests entered the Courtyard and circled in opposite directions, checking every sacred vessel, meeting at the Chamber of the Bakers with the declaration: "Everything is at peace." On Sabbath eve, torches were replaced by the candles lit before the Sabbath.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 8
👑
Honor Through Presence
Guarding the Temple is an act of reverence, not security — just as a palace with guards is more impressive than one without, constant watchfulness expresses the Temple's supreme dignity.
⚖️
Two Sides of One Mitzvah
Standing guard fulfills a positive commandment from the Torah, while abandoning the watch transgresses a negative commandment — making vigilance over the Sanctuary a complete halakhic obligation.
🤝
Priestly-Levitical Partnership
Priests guarded from inside the Courtyard and Levites from outside, together forming a unified wall of sacred service derived from three distinct Torah verses.
🌄
Nightly Watch as Exile and Redemption
The nightly guard during exile — and the study of its laws — parallels our vigil for the dawn of the Messianic era, when the light kindled in darkness will illuminate the service of the Third Temple.
