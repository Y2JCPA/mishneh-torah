🏺 Sacred Continuity
The Ark was placed in the western portion of the Holy of Holies, alongside the vial of manna and Aharon's staff. When destruction loomed, Solomon built hidden vaults beneath the Temple, and King Josiah entombed the Ark there — along with the anointing oil — ensuring the Shechinah's presence was never severed from the Temple Mount. Because the Ark remained buried below, the site's holiness endured through exile and remains intact to this day.

Dividing the Holy: Wall, Curtains, and the Cubit of Uncertainty

הלכה ב׳
הלכה ב׳
בְּבַיִת רִאשׁוֹן הָיָה כֹּתֶל מַבְדִּיל בֵּין הַקֹּדֶשׁ וּבֵין קֹדֶשׁ הַקָּדָשִׁים עָבְיוֹ אַמָּה. וְכֵיוָן שֶׁבָּנוּ הַבַּיִת שֵׁנִי נִסְתַּפֵּק לָהֶם אִם עֳבִי הַכֹּתֶל הָיָה מִמִּדַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ אוֹ מִמִּדַּת קֹדֶשׁ הַקָּדָשִׁים לְפִיכָךְ עָשׂוּ קֹדֶשׁ הַקָּדָשִׁים עָבְיוֹ עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה תְּמִימוֹת וְעָשׂוּ הַקֹּדֶשׁ אַרְבָּעִים אַמָּה תְּמִימוֹת וְהִנִּיחוּ אַמָּה יְתֵרָה בֵּין הַקֹּדֶשׁ וּבֵין קֹדֶשׁ הַקָּדָשִׁים וְלֹא בָּנוּ כֹּתֶל בְּבַיִת שֵׁנִי אֶלָּא עָשׂוּ שְׁתֵּי פָּרוֹכוֹת אַחַת מִצַּד קֹדֶשׁ הַקָּדָשִׁים וְאַחַת מִצַּד הַקֹּדֶשׁ וּבֵינֵיהֶן אַמָּה כְּנֶגֶד עֳבִי הַכֹּתֶל שֶׁהָיָה בָּרִאשׁוֹן. אֲבָל בְּמִקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן לֹא הָיְתָה שָׁם אֶלָּא פָרֹכֶת אַחַת בִּלְבַד שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (שמות כו לג) "וְהִבְדִּילָה הַפָּרֹכֶת לָכֶם" וְגוֹ':
The First Temple had a one-cubit thick wall which separated the Sanctuary and the Holy of Holies.12As the Rambam states in the conclusion of the halachah, a divider was necessary between the sanctity of the Holy of Holies and the Sanctuary. In the Tabernacle in the desert, a curtain, the parochet, alone served this function. In the First Temple, a wall was also built in addition to the curtain. When the Second Temple was constructed, they were unsure whether the width of that wall was included in the measure of the Sanctuary or the Holy of Holies.13I Kings 6:2 states: "And the length of the Temple Solomon built was 60 cubits." The narrative continues (ibid.:17, 20): "The Sanctuary was 40 cubits long...and the space for the ark was 20 cubits long." Since the combined length of both chambers was only 60 cubits, the width of the wall had to be included in the measure of one of the chambers. However, it was not clear from which chamber it should be built (Jerusalem Talmud, Kelayim 8:4; see also Yoma 52a for a slightly different explanation). Therefore, the Holy of Holies was made a full twenty cubits long, and the Sanctuary a full forty cubits long, and one additional cubit was left between the Sanctuary and the Holy of Holies.14The Marginita D'Rabbi Meir asks why the builders of the Second Temple were willing to add an extra cubit between the two chambers, when they hesitated to increase the width of the dividing wall. In resolution, it is explained that originally the eastern wall built by Solomon was seven cubits thick, while in the Second Temple, it was only six cubits thick. Thus, there was no change in the total length of the Temple building.They did not build a wall in the Second Temple.15Bava Batra 3a,b explains that they did not build a wall because the Second Temple was higher than the First. The First Temple was only 30 cubits high. The Second Temple was 100 cubits high. A wall only a cubit thick would not be sturdy if built to such a height. Nevertheless, the width of the wall was not increased, because its original width was established by Ruach HaKodesh, Divine revelation. Thus, they returned to the pattern established in the Sanctuary of Moses and utilized curtains as dividers. Rather, they hung two curtains, one from the side of the Sanctuary and one from the side of the Holy of Holies, with a cubit between them16Yoma 52b relates that the external curtain had an opening on the south and the inner curtain had an opening on the north. Thus, to enter the Holy of Holies, the High Priest had to go through the entire width of the Temple between the two curtains. in place of the width of the wall of the First [Temple].17Tosafot (Yoma, ibid.) asks why they were not able to resolve their dilemma by hanging one curtain, a cubit in width.In response, the commentary states that both curtains were necessary because the verse quoted above, "the curtain shall divide..." clearly implies that the sanctity of the Holy of Holies begins with the outer surface of the curtain. Thus, based on the possibility that the wall of the First Temple was included within the 20 cubits of the Holy of Holies, an additional cubit would have been included in this sacred area if only one curtain was used. From this perspective, the inner curtain had to be a distinct entity, marking the beginning of the most sacred chamber.Conversely, based on the view that the wall had been included in the measure of the Sanctuary and that the wall and the Holy of Holies encompassed 21 cubits, a thick curtain would have been unnecessary, since the outer curtain was located where the division was required to be made. However, in the First Temple, there was only one curtain,18The Kessef Mishneh explains that the expression "First Temple" refers to the Sanctuary, for the First Temple itself had a wall rather than a curtain. However, Rav Yaakov Emden disputes this issue, quoting Yoma 54a which brings a number of Aggadot concerning the Ark and the curtain in the First Temple. The Rambam's Commentary to the Mishnah (Middot 4:7) specifically states that there was a curtain in addition to the wall in the First Temple. as [Exodus 26:33] states: "The curtain will divide for you [between the Sanctuary and the Holy of Holies.]"
🪡 One Cubit, Two Worlds
The First Temple had a one-cubit wall separating the Sanctuary from the Holy of Holies. When the Second Temple was built, the builders could not determine whether that cubit belonged to the Sanctuary or the Holy of Holies. Their elegant solution: measure both chambers at their full original dimensions and leave one cubit between them, filled not by a wall but by two curtains — one facing each sacred space — reflecting the Second Temple's greater height and its return to the Mosaic pattern.

📐 Holy Geometry
The Second Temple formed a perfect cube of 100 cubits in every direction. Its height was layered: a 6-cubit base, a 40-cubit Sanctuary, ornate ceilings, drainage spaces (Beit Dilpa), an upper storey, a guardrail, and an iron bird-deterrent called the Kaleh Orev. East to west and north to south, the same total was achieved through nested double walls, cells, curtain-gaps, chambers, and entrances — each cubit Divinely precise and Halachically significant.

🦁 Enter with Awe
The Temple's entrance complex combined grandeur with sacred restriction. The great gate was 10 cubits wide and 20 cubits high, with four doors. The soaring Entrance Hall opening measured 40 cubits high and 20 wide, crowned by five escalating oak beams. Two small wickets flanked the gate — the southern one never opened, as Ezekiel foretold. Balconies ringed the building on three sides, and projections covered the walls to keep priests from leaning irreverently — the whole structure shaped like a lion: wide at the front, narrowing to the rear.

🌀 Hidden Passages
Between the Temple's nested double walls lay 38 cells across three levels — fifteen on the north, fifteen on the south, and eight on the west — used for storage. Each cell connected to its neighbors through multiple entrances; a special cell in the northeast corner of the second storey had five entrances. A winding stairwell ascended counterclockwise from northeast to the upper storey's entrance on the south. Above, cedar beams led to the roof; craftsmen were lowered through apertures into the Holy of Holies in closed boxes so they would not gaze upon its sanctity. The entire building was coated with cement once a year, from Passover to Passover.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 4
🏺
The Ark Anchors Eternal Holiness
By entombing the Ark in hidden vaults beneath the Temple Mount, Solomon ensured the Shechinah's presence — and thus the site's sanctity — would never be nullified, binding together the Tabernacle, both Temples, and the future Messianic Temple in one unbroken sacred thread.
📐
Every Cubit Is Divinely Mandated
The Temple's dimensions — 100 cubits in every direction, each cubit accounted for through walls, cells, curtains, and chambers — were not merely architectural choices but Halachically ordained measurements rooted in Ruach HaKodesh, which is why even the Second Temple builders would not alter the cubit-width of the dividing wall.
🚪
Access Is Structured, Not Open
From the eternally-sealed southern wicket to the craftsmen lowered in boxes so they could not gaze freely at the Holy of Holies, the Temple's design encodes the principle that proximity to the Divine must be earned, regulated, and approached with deliberate reverence.
🌀
Complexity Serves Sanctity
The Temple's maze of nested walls, cells, stairwells, and balconies was not architectural excess but purposeful sacred engineering — each layer adding protection, separation, and awe, ensuring that even the workers who maintained the building could not casually enter or exit its most holy precincts.
