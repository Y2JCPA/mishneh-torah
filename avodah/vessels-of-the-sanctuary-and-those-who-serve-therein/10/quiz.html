לרפואת פייגא בת יטא רבקה

🎓 Quiz

הלכות כלי המקדש פרק י · 5 Questions
Question 1
What is the very first garment a priest must put on, and at what height is the sash tied?
Despite a verse mentioning the tunic first, rabbinic exegesis (Yoma 23b) establishes that leggings are always put on before anything else. The sash is tied at elbow height — 'above the heart' — not at the armpits or loins where perspiration occurs (Ezekiel 44:18).
Question 2
If an ordinary priest performs Temple service while wearing only three of his four required garments, what is the legal consequence?
A priest who serves with fewer than his required garments is considered 'lacking garments,' his service is retroactively invalid, and he is liable for death at the hand of Heaven — just like a non-priest who performs Temple service.
Question 3
A priest wraps a cloth around his heel (an area not covered by priestly garments). At what minimum size does this cloth constitute a disqualifying interposition?
A cloth of three fingerbreadths by three fingerbreadths — the halachic minimum size of a significant garment — constitutes an interposition and invalidates service. Smaller than that, it is not considered a substance of consequence. However, a small belt disqualifies regardless of size because it is treated as an independent garment.
Question 4
Why was the Urim and Tumim not consulted during the Second Temple period, even though it was physically present?
The Rambam explains that the Urim and Tumim function only when the Holy Spirit (ruach hakodesh) rests upon the High Priest. In the Second Temple era, this divine presence was absent — one of the five differences between the two Temples — so while the physical object existed, consultation was meaningless.
Question 5
Who was permitted to make inquiry of the Urim and Tumim, and how many matters could be asked at one time?
Based on Numbers 27:21, inquiry was restricted to the king ('he'), those the entire people depended upon ('all the children of Israel'), and the High Court ('all the congregation'). Ordinary individuals could not inquire. Furthermore, only one matter could be asked at a time — if two were asked simultaneously, only the first received an answer.
