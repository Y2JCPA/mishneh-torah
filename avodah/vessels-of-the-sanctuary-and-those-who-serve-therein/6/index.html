The Institution of Ma'amadot: Origins and Structure

הלכות א׳–ב׳
הלכה א׳
אִי אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁיִּהְיֶה קָרְבָּנוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קָרֵב וְהוּא אֵינוֹ עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבָּיו. וְקָרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּבּוּר הֵן קָרְבָּן שֶׁל כָּל יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁיִּהְיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל כֻּלָּן עוֹמְדִין בָּעֲזָרָה בִּשְׁעַת קָרְבָּן. לְפִיכָךְ תִּקְּנוּ נְבִיאִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים שֶׁיִּבְרְרוּ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל כְּשֵׁרִים וִירֵאֵי חֵטְא וְיִהְיוּ שְׁלוּחֵי כָּל יִשְׂרָאֵל לַעֲמֹד עַל הַקָּרְבָּנוֹת וְהֵם הַנִּקְרָאִים אַנְשֵׁי מַעֲמָד. וְחִלְּקוּ אוֹתָם כ"ד מַעֲמָדוֹת כְּמִנְיַן מִשְׁמְרוֹת כְּהֻנָּה וּלְוִיָּה. וְעַל כָּל מַעֲמָד וּמַעֲמָד אֶחָד מֵהֶן מְמֻנֶּה עַל כֻּלָּם וְהוּא נִקְרָא רֹאשׁ הַמַּעֲמָד:
It is impossible for the sacrifice of a person to be offered without him standing in attendance. [Now,] the communal offerings are the sacrifices of the entire Jewish people, but it is impossible for the entire Jewish people to stand in the Temple Courtyard at the time they are being offered. Therefore, the prophets of the first era1Samuel and David (Ta'anit 27a). ordained that there be selective upright and sin-fearing Jews who should serve as the agents of the entire Jewish people2In his Commentary to the Mishnah (Ta'anit 4:2), the Rambam writes: " Their intent and their goal was involvement in Divine service and prayer. They were not occupied with their own concerns. Their minds and their thought were on the sacrifices." to stand [and observe the offering of] the sacrifices. They were called "the men of the maamad."3Ma'amad literally means "standing," because they would stand over the sacrifice or stand in prayer, as stated in Halachah 5. The term also has the connotation of "status" and "dignified position."They divided them into 24 ma'amadot, equaling the number of watches of the priests and Levites. Over each of the watches was one who was appointed as [the supervisor] of them all. He was called the head of the ma'amad.
הלכה ב׳
בְּכָל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת מִתְקַבְּצִין אַנְשֵׁי מַעֲמָד שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ שַׁבָּת. מִי שֶׁהָיָה מֵהֶן בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם אוֹ קָרוֹב לָהּ נִכְנָסִין לַמִּקְדָּשׁ עִם מִשְׁמַר כְּהֻנָּה וּלְוִיָּה שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ שַׁבָּת. וְהָרְחוֹקִים שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ מַעֲמָד כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ מַעֲמָד שֶׁלָּהֶן הֵן מִתְקַבְּצִין לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת שֶׁבִּמְקוֹמָן:
Each week, the members of the ma'amad of that week would gather together. Those [living] in Jerusalem or close to it would enter the Temple with the priestly and Levitical watch of that week. When [the week of] their ma'amad arrived, those members of the ma'amad who [lived] in distant places would gather in the synagogues of their locale.
🏛️ Sacred Delegation
Since the entire Jewish people cannot stand in the Temple Courtyard during communal offerings, Samuel and David ordained 24 ma'amadot — groups of select, sin-fearing Jews who serve as agents of the whole nation. Those near Jerusalem entered the Temple itself; those far away gathered in local synagogues, linking every Jew to the sacrificial service.

Fasting, Prayer, and the Fourfold Daily Regimen

הלכות ג׳–ה׳
הלכה ג׳
וּמַה הֵן עוֹשִׂין אֵלּוּ הַמִּתְקַבְּצִין בֵּין בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם בֵּין בְּבָתֵּי כְּנֵסִיּוֹת. מִתְעַנִּין בְּשֵׁנִי בַּשַּׁבָּת שֶׁלָּהֶן. וּבִשְׁלִישִׁי וּבִרְבִיעִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי. אֲבָל בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת לֹא הָיוּ מִתְעַנִּין מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד שַׁבָּת וּבְאֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת אֵינָם מִתְעַנִּים כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יֵצְאוּ מֵעֹנֶג שַׁבָּת לְצוֹם:
What would those who gather together - both in Jerusalem and in the synagogues - do? They would fast on the Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday of their week. They would not fast on Friday out of respect for the Sabbath4So that they would have the energy to make their Sabbath preparations. and not on Sunday, so they would not make a transition from the pleasure of the Sabbath to a fast.5This would weaken them exceedingly (ibid.).
הלכה ד׳
וּבְכָל יוֹם וְיוֹם מִשַּׁבָּת שֶׁהָיָה מַעֲמָדָן מִתְפַּלְּלִין אַרְבַּע תְּפִלּוֹת שַׁחֲרִית וּמִנְחָה וּנְעִילָה וְעוֹד מוֹסִיפִין תְּפִלָּה אַחֶרֶת בֵּין שַׁחֲרִית וּמִנְחָה וְהִיא יְתֵרָה לָהֶן. וְנוֹשְׂאִין כַּפֵּיהֶן הַכֹּהֲנִים בַּמַעֲמָד שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים בְּכָל יוֹם בְּשַׁחֲרִית וּבַתְּפִלָּה זוֹ הַיְתֵרָה וּבִנְעִילָה. וְקוֹרִין בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שְׁלֹשָׁה אֲנָשִׁים שְׁתֵּי פְּעָמִים בְּכָל יוֹם בְּשַׁחֲרִית וּבַתְּפִלָּה הַשְּׁנִיָּה שֶׁמּוֹסִיפִין אֲבָל בְּמִנְחָה לֹא הָיוּ קוֹרִין בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא עַל פֶּה כְּקוֹרְאִין אֶת שְׁמַע. וְלֹא הָיוּ מִתְקַבְּצִין לִתְפִלַּת מִנְחָה בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן טְרוּדִין לְשַׁבָּת:
On every day of the week that was their ma'amad, they would recite four prayer services: the morning service, the afternoon service, the neilah service,6Neilah means "closing." Hilchot Tefilah 1:7 states that the Men of the Great Assembly ordained "a prayer after the afternoon service [to be recited] close to sunset on fast days only to increase supplication and pleading due to the fast. This is called the neilah service, as if to say the gates of heaven are being closed." Since the men of the ma'amad were observing an ordained fast, they would recite this service as well. and another prayer service between the morning service and the afternoon service which was additional [and unique] for them.7The Ra'avad differs and maintains that there was not an extra service ordained especially for the men of the ma'amad. If the Mishnah spoke of Musaf, the additional service, in this context, the intent was a day like Rosh Chodesh when Musaf would be recited by the entire Jewish people.The Radbaz supports the Rambam's opinion, explaining that during this prayer service they would pray that the sacrifices be accepted and that Jews in situations of danger be saved. In the ma'amad, the priests bless the people three times, in the morning service, in this additional service, and in the neilah service.8They would not bless the people in the afternoon service, because generally, it was suspected that a priest might be intoxicated in the afternoon and it is improper to bless the people in such a state. Hence, our Sages forbade the recitation of the priestly blessing in the afternoon service at all times. They allowed it to be recited in the Neilah service, because the neilah service is only recited on a fast day. In later generations, it became customary to recite the priestly blessing (or in the Ashkenazic community, the passage Eloheinu V'Elohei Avoseinu) in the afternoon service, because at present, it is customary to recite the afternoon service late on a fast day. Hence, it resembles the neilah service and will not be confused with an ordinary afternoon service. See the Rambam's Commentary to the Mishnah (Taanit 4:1), Hilchot Nesiat Kapayim 14:1-2.They would have three men read from the Torah twice a day: in the morning service and in the second service that they would add.9The Ra'avad differs here as well and states that there is no extra service and, hence, no extra Torah reading, except on a day when Musaf is recited. In the afternoon service, they would not read the Torah. Instead, they would recite [the passages] by heart as one recites the Shema. They would not gather for the afternoon service on Friday, for they were busy [with their preparations] for the Sabbath.
הלכה ה׳
וְקִבּוּצָן לְכָל תְּפִלָּה מֵאַרְבַּע תְּפִלּוֹת אֵלּוּ וַעֲמִידָתָן שָׁם לִתְפִלָּה וְלִתְחִנָּה וּלְבַקָּשָׁה וְלִקְרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה נִקְרָא מַעֲמָד:
Their gathering together for each of these prayers of these four services and their standing in prayer, supplication, and petition and reading the Torah is called a ma'amad.
🕯️ Days of Devotion
Throughout their week of service, the men of the ma'amad fasted Monday through Thursday — neither on Friday (to preserve Sabbath energy) nor on Sunday (to avoid an abrupt transition from Sabbath joy to deprivation). Each day they recited four prayer services — morning, an extra service unique to them, afternoon, and neilah — with the priestly blessing offered three times. This gathering in prayer and Torah reading is itself called a ma'amad.

Torah Readings: Creation, Scrolls, and Suspended Days

הלכות ו׳–ח׳
הלכה ו׳
וּבַמֶּה הָיוּ קוֹרְאִין בְּמַעֲשֵׂה בְּרֵאשִׁית. בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן קוֹרְאִין בְּרֵאשִׁית וִיהִי רָקִיעַ. בַּשֵּׁנִי יְהִי רָקִיעַ וְיִקָּווּ. בִּשְׁלִישִׁי יִקָּווּ וִיהִי מְאֹרֹת. בִּרְבִיעִי (בראשית א יד) "יְהִי מְאֹרֹת" (בראשית א כ) "וְיִשְׁרְצוּ". בַּחֲמִישִׁי יִשְׁרְצוּ (בראשית א כד) "וְתוֹצֵא הָאָרֶץ". בְּשִׁשִּׁי תּוֹצֵא הָאָרֶץ (בראשית ב א) "וַיְכֻלּוּ":
What would they read [from the Torah]? The narrative of creation.10In his Commentary to the Mishnah (loc. cit.:2), the Rambam explains that this subject was chosen, because the ultimate fulfillment of existence is the service of sacrificial worship, as implied by Ta'anit 27b: "Were it not for the sacrifices, the heavens and the earth would not be maintained." On the first day, they would read: "In the beginning," "Let there be a firmament;"11On each day, they would read the passage associated with that and the following day of creation. On the second day, "Let there be a firmament" and "Let [the waters] be gathered;" on the third day, "Let [the waters] be gathered" and "Let there be luminaries;" on the fourth day, "Let there be luminaries" and "Let the waters teem;" on the fifth day, "Let the waters teem" and "Let the earth produce;" on the sixth day, "Let the earth produce" and "[The heavens and the earth] were completed."
הלכה ז׳
פָּרָשָׁה גְּדוֹלָה קוֹרְאִין אוֹתָהּ בִּשְׁנַיִם וּקְטַנָּה קוֹרֵא אוֹתָהּ אֶחָד. וּשְׁתֵּי פָּרָשִׁיּוֹת שֶׁקּוֹרְאִין שַׁחֲרִית הֵם שֶׁחוֹזְרִין וְקוֹרִין אוֹתָן בִּתְפִלָּה הַשְּׁנִיָּה בַּסֵּפֶר. וְחוֹזְרִין וְקוֹרְאִין אוֹתָן בַּמִּנְחָה עַל פֶּה:
A large passage12I.e., three aliyot were given out for each reading. If the passage was large - i.e., it contained eight verses - two aliyot were given in it and the third aliyah was given for the second passage that accompanied it. would be read by two men, a small passage, by one. The two passages that were read in the morning would be read during this second service from a scroll. They would then be read again during the afternoon service by heart.
הלכה ח׳
שְׁמוֹנַת יְמֵי חֲנֻכָּה לֹא הָיוּ אַנְשֵׁי מַעֲמָד עוֹשִׂין מַעֲמָד בְּשַׁחֲרִית. וְכָל יוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ קָרְבַּן מוּסָף לֹא הָיָה בּוֹ מַעֲמָד לֹא בִּתְפִלָּה שְׁנִיָּה שֶׁלָּהֶם וְלֹא בַּמִּנְחָה. אֶלָּא בְּשַׁחֲרִית וּבִנְעִילָה בִּלְבַד. וְכָל יוֹם שֶׁהָיָה בּוֹ קָרְבַּן הָעֵצִים לֹא הָיָה בּוֹ מַעֲמָד בִּנְעִילָה אֶלָּא בְּשַׁחֲרִית וּבִתְפִלָּה שְׁנִיָּה וּבְמִנְחָה:
During the eight days of Chanukah, the men of the ma'amad would not carry out a ma'amad13I.e., read from the Torah or recite the readings by heart [the Rambam's Commentary to the Mishnah (loc. cit.:4)]. in the morning service.14Since they would recite the Hallel prayers that day. As Taanit 4:4 states: Whenever Hallel was recited [and the Musaf service was not recited (the Rambam's Commentary to the Mishnah)], there was no ma'amad. The rationale is that since the recitation of Hallel took time, no further obligations were imposed on the men of the ma'amad. On any day when there was a Musaf sacrifice,15I.e., on the intermediate days of the festivals and on Rosh Chodesh. there was not a ma'amad during their second service, nor in the afternoon service,16For they were occupied with the sacrifice of the Musaf service. only during the morning and neilah services. On any day when there was a sacrifice of wood,17See the following halachah. there was not a ma'amad during the neilah service, only during the morning, second, and afternoon services.
📜 Genesis & the Altar
The ma'amad read the narrative of Creation — because the entire purpose of creation finds its fulfillment in the sacrificial service. Each day's reading paired that day's creative act with the next. Large passages received two readers; small ones, one. On Chanukah (when Hallel is recited), the morning ma'amad reading was suspended; on days with a Musaf sacrifice, the second and afternoon services were suspended; on wood-offering days, only the neilah ma'amad was suspended.

The Wood Offering, Family Festivals, and Personal Conduct

הלכות ט׳–י״א
הלכה ט׳
וּמַהוּ קָרְבַּן הָעֵצִים. זְמַן קָבוּעַ הָיָה לְמִשְׁפָּחוֹת מִשְׁפָּחוֹת לָצֵאת לַיְעָרִים לְהָבִיא עֵצִים לַמַּעֲרָכָה. וְיוֹם שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לִבְנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה זוֹ לְהָבִיא הָעֵצִים הָיוּ מַקְרִיבִין עוֹלוֹת נְדָבָה וְזֶהוּ קָרְבַּן הָעֵצִים וְהָיָה לָהֶם כְּמוֹ יוֹם טוֹב. וַאֲסוּרִין בּוֹ בְּהֶסְפֵּד וּבְתַעֲנִית וּבַעֲשִׂיַּת מְלָאכָה וְדָבָר זֶה מִנְהָג:
What was a sacrifice of wood? Certain families had a fixed time on which they would go out to the forests and bring wood for the arrangement [on the altar].18In his Commentary to the Mishnah (loc. cit.:5), the Rambam refers to Nechemiah 10:35 which speaks of casting lots for the wood offering. Implied is that different families were allotted the responsibility for bringing wood for the altar and were given different days to bring that wood. On that day, in addition to the wood, they would bring other sacrifices, as the Rambam continues to explain. On the day designated for this family to bring their sacrifices, they would bring voluntary burnt offerings. This was called the sacrifice of the wood. It was like a festival for these families and they were forbidden to have eulogies delivered, fast, and perform work on that day. This was a custom.
הלכה י׳
אֲפִלּוּ יָחִיד שֶׁהִתְנַדֵּב עֵצִים אוֹ גְּזָרִים בַּמַּעֲרָכָה אָסוּר בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם בְּהֶסְפֵּד וּבְתַעֲנִית וּבַעֲשִׂיַּת מְלָאכָה וְדָבָר זֶה מִנְהָג:
Even a private individual who gave wood or logs for the arrangement [of the altar]19For bringing wood is considered equivalent to bringing a sacrifice and the day on which a person brings a sacrifice is considered as his private festival. is forbidden to have eulogies delivered, fast, and perform work on that day. This was a custom.
הלכה י״א׳
אַנְשֵׁי מַעֲמָד אֲסוּרִין מִלְּסַפֵּר וּמִלְּכַבֵּס כָּל שַׁבָּת שֶׁלָּהֶן. וּבַחֲמִישִׁי מֻתָּרִין מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד הַשַּׁבָּת. וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אָסְרוּ עֲלֵיהֶם לְסַפֵּר וּלְכַבֵּס כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִכָּנְסוּ לְמַעֲמָדָם כְּשֶׁהֵם מְנֻוָּלִין. אֶלָּא יְסַפְּרוּ וִיכַבְּסוּ מִקֹּדֶם:
The men of the ma'amad are forbidden to have their hair cut and to launder [their clothes] throughout the week [they serve in the Temple]. On Thursday,20And certainly on Friday (Rambam LeAm). they were permitted in honor of the Sabbath. Why were they forbidden to have their hair cut and to launder [their clothes]? So that they would not enter their ma'amad while they were unkept. Instead, they would have their hair cut and launder [their clothes] beforehand.21See Hilchot Bi'at HaMikdash 1:17,12.
🪵 Sacred Wood, Sacred Dignity
Designated families brought wood to the altar on their appointed days, accompanied by voluntary burnt offerings — a personal festival on which eulogies, fasting, and work were forbidden, even for a private individual who donated wood. Men of the ma'amad were forbidden from cutting hair or laundering clothes during their service week — not as deprivation, but to ensure they arrived already groomed, entering the Temple in dignity rather than scrambling to prepare mid-week.

🎓 Key Principles

Chapter 6
🤝
Communal Responsibility Through Representation
When the entire people cannot fulfill a mitzvah directly, they appoint worthy representatives whose devotion enacts the service on behalf of every Jew.
⚡
Fasting as Spiritual Solidarity with the Altar
The men of the ma'amad fasted while the communal sacrifices were offered, forging a bond between the people's self-deprivation and the daily service that sustains the world.
🌍
Creation's Purpose Is Fulfilled at the Altar
Reading the story of Creation during the ma'amad expressed the teaching that were it not for the sacrifices, heaven and earth would not be maintained — the Temple is the world's axis.
👔
Entering Sacred Service in Dignity, Not Haste
The prohibitions on haircuts and laundering during the service week ensured that the men of the ma'amad arrived prepared and dignified, not making themselves presentable at the last moment.
